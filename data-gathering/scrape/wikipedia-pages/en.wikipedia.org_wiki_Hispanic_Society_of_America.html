The Hispanic Society of America is a museum of Spanish, Portuguese, and Latin American art and artifacts, as well as a rare books and manuscripts research library. Founded in 1904 by Archer M. Huntington, the institution is free and open to the public at its original location in a Beaux Arts building on Audubon Terrace (at 155th Street and Broadway) in the lower Washington Heights area of New York City in the United States.

Exterior sculpture at the Society includes work by Anna Hyatt Huntington and nine major reliefs by the Swiss-American sculptor Berthold Nebel, a commission that took ten years to complete.

[edit] Museum collections

The museum contains works by Diego Velázquez, Francisco de Goya, El Greco, and Joaquín Sorolla y Bastida, among others.

A major component of this museum is the Sorolla Room which was reinstalled in 2010. It displays a massive series of Sorolla paintings created from 1911 to 1919 (commissioned by Archer Huntington). The paintings ring the large room (estimate: 50 ft square) and depict scenes from each of the provinces of Spain.

The rare books library maintains 15,000 books printed before 1700, including a first edition of Don Quijote.

[edit] Recent news

NEW YORK, NY, May 6, 2010 – One of New York City’s most magnificent site-specific artworks, and one of the greatest monuments of 20th-century Spanish art, Joaquín Sorolla’s Visión de España (Vision of Spain) will return to its home at The Hispanic Society of America on May 7, 2010, following an unprecedented tour of Spanish museums. While on tour, Visión de España was viewed by over two million people, making it the most visited exhibition in the history of Spain.

Created in 1911-1919 expressly for the Hispanic Society, Visión de España comprises 14 canvases representing the people, landscapes and customs of nine regions of Spain. Realized through staggering effort by the most renowned and popular Spanish artist of his era, who insisted on composing each painting from life sketches made in the open air, the ensemble as a whole measures approximately 230 feet wide (70 meters) and is approximately 11.5 feet high (3.5 meters) , with figures depicted at full life size.

After being installed at the Hispanic Society in 1926, around the walls of what is now known as the Bancaja Gallery, Visión de España remained in place for more than eighty years. Then, in 2007, the Hispanic Society initiated the master plan for its landmark building complex on Manhattan’s Audubon Terrace, beginning with a much-needed renovation and subtle redesign of the Sorolla Gallery, along with a renovation and reinstallation of the adjacent, richly decorated Main Court.

Taking advantage of the construction period for the renovation, the Hispanic Society painstakingly restored Visión de España at a cost of $250,000 and organized the first tour of the masterwork to Spanish museums, with both undertakings being generously supported by Fundación Bancaja, Valencia.

“Now that the crowning work of Sorolla’s career has at last been shown in Spain, where it had never been seen before, we are thrilled to welcome it back home, knowing that our visitors, too, may feel as if they are seeing it for the first time,” stated Mitchell A. Codding, Executive Director of the Hispanic Society of America. “Beautiful details that had been obscured over the years have been uncovered by our restoration. But, more important, our renovation and reinstallation of the Sorolla Gallery has dramatically improved the lighting, removed intrusive furnishings and corrected the angle of vision between the viewer and the paintings. The experience of Visión de España is now truly more glorious and overwhelming than it has ever been before.”

Attendance at the museum is typically sparse although it is located near the Cloisters, Dyckman House Museum and Manhattan's oldest mansion, the Morris-Jumel Mansion Museum.
