      Chris G.

      Brooklyn, NY

      9/14/2009

      This refers to the library section of BHS: We dropped by to look up info about our house. BHS's library has info you can't find anywhere else (the Dept of Buildings had next to no info we could use). But the library has atlases dating back to the mid 1800's, deed info, resident directories, old articles and such.

      Technically you're supposed to make an appointment to visit the library, but it apparently wasn't a busy day when we were there, so they said to come on up. The library itself is gorgeous, all old wood and big tables. The librarians were really helpful, digging up stuff about the neighborhood and finding stuff we never would have thought to look up. We found out a lot of interesting stuff out about our house, although we'll need to return to really get a complete picture.

      That's my only complaint about the place, accessibility. It's only open til 5 from Wednesday to Saturday, and it's a $6 charge. Obviously this means most regular folk will never be able to get in there without making special plans for it (which is what we did). But otherwise it's a wonderful resource which I plan to mine further.

      Review from Tom K.

      Tom K.

      5/16/2010

      Before there was Brooklyn, there was Breukelen, a land of Dutch colonists, farmland and Lenape locales.

      "Pages of the Past, The Adventures of Jasper Danckaerts" is a current exhibition at the BHS that brings diary entries to life with original sketches from the 1670's along with maps. It was a fascinating look into pre-English, pre-American Brooklyn and offered historical nuggets such as Brooklyn remaining Dutch for about 10 years after Manhattan.

      The BHS building dates back to the 1870's and is itself an impressive structure to study. Three floors of exhibits can be visited by elevator or staircase. Walls along the staircase are decorated with paintings, including one of George Washington (Battle of Brooklyn and all) along with other historical personalities who lent their name to various parts of the borough.

      Where BHS falls short, however, is in the size and scope of the exhibits. I was disappointed that more artifacts from archaeological digs or personal collections were not on display. Case in point-- there was a single room dedicated to various eras of BK history: dutch/native american, colonial, civil war, ww II. The stories presented were definitely broad, very broad, strokes of history. There were a few items (A Singer sewing machine) but compared to other such institutions I'd thought there would simply be more "stuff".

      There is ample use of sound bites to bring to life voices of the past. In a section dedicated to Vietnam Vets, you can hear their stories by placing your feet on the imprints of steps on the floor. The lobby level also included a photo exhibition on the current residents of Tivoli Towers, adding a dash of the contemporary to a historical society.

      You'll find various brochures in the lobby dealing with other local attractions. I recommend picking up the walking tour of Brooklyn Heights which highlights the various buildings of note in the immediate area. As for the BHS, it's a comfortable space to visit and learn a bit of borough history. Some of that space just seems underutilized at the moment.

    About This Business

    Provided by business

    Specialties

    Visit the Brooklyn Historical Society for museum exhibits and public programs that Brooklyn's history to life. Research your family, home or scholarly interest in the gorgeous Othmer library.

    The magnificent 1881 building by architect George B. Post (who designed the New York Stock Exchange building among others) is landmarked for its exterior and interior.

    The mission of the Brooklyn Historical Society is to connect the past to the present and make the vvibrant history of Brooklyn tangible, relevant, and meaningful for today's diverse communities, and for generations to come.

    History

    Established in 1863

    The Brooklyn Historical Society was founded in 1863 as the Long Island Historical Society. At that time, the city of Brooklyn was the commercial and cultural center of Long Island. During World War I, BHS contributed to the war effort by transforming its 600-seat auditorium into a Red Cross headquarters by removing the seats and building a flat floor over the original sloping floor.

    In the 1980's, new leadership reestablished the organization as a museum and education center. In 1989 BHS restored its ground floor. Today, BHS welcomes visitors to the museum and library.

    BHS has the most comprehensive collection of Brooklyn-related materials in existence. In 1993, the U.S. Department of Education designated its library as a "major research library" under Title II-C of the Higher Education Act. BHS is committed to offering programming that helps Brooklynites young and old develop pride in their own cultural traditions while fostering understanding of their neighbors.
