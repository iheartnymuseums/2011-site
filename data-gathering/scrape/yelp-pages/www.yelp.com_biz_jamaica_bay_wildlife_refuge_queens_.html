      Review from Marcus V.

       

      62friends

      301reviews

      Marcus V.

      Brooklyn, NY

      3/13/2010 5 photos

      If you love nature, enjoy walking and exploring, and into bird photography, Jamaica Bay Wildlife Refuge is NYC's avian hot spot. Best time to visit and hike the trails is in the autumn, preferably late autumn when many birds are stopping through as they migrate south and the weather has turned cooler. I made the mistake of coming here in the summer once, during August heat and humidity, and if wasn't pleasant. Due to such conditions, you'll see a limited number of birds because they'll be keeping to the shade. Pre-dusk or very early morning are best times in the summer. Spring is also an ideal time to visit, as it's another migratory season.

      You'll never know what you'll see. One time I caught a glimpse of a black crown night heron. I barely missed it as it sat in a distant tree cloaked by the shadow of the canopy. It just sat there and watched  me after it realized that I was staring at him. I saw a lot of cormorants, terns, geese (a given to see routinely), white egrets, and so many other species.  

      Simply a great place to bring your camera if you're serious about nature photography. It's beautiful here, very serene, and a skip and a beat away from Brooklyn and the city.

      Judy K.

      Jackson Heights, NY

      7/12/2009 4 photos

      Do you know there's a wildlife refuge in Queens?   I've lived here all my life and just recently found out about it in Time Out  NY.   I was like, really?  REALLY?  Time to check it out!  There are two trails you can take to explore the area, one is dry and the other is marshy.  They each wrap around a different pond.  The dry one, the west pond trail, is an easy 2-mile walk on a gravel path.   Hubby and I got here right when the park opened and there were already a bunch of other crazy early birds walking this trail.  We liberally doused ourselves with bug spray because yes, you will encounter ticks and mosquitoes, each one a little vampire waiting to ambush you.

      Along the trail, we passed by an elevated nest occupied by three ospreys.   Definitely have your binoculars out so you can see this happy family.  Another highlight was this beach covered with a gazillion geese sunbathing nonchalantly like they were on the Riviera.  They were quite loud, even from afar, and a lone egret? ibis? crane? stood among them.   We saw many other birds, but I couldn't tell you heads or tails what they were.  We are city folk after all, the kind who go, "Look!  A red one just flew past!"  Suffice it to say there were a good variety of birds to be seen.   Normally, there are sea turtles to ogle too, but it's nesting season right now and they hung out a Do Not Disturb sign by their place.

      Review from Heath R.

       

      189friends

      838reviews

      Heath R.

      Los Angeles, CA

      8/3/2009

      One of my absolute favorite places in the city. It's an easy subway ride to the Broad Channel stop on the A -- and then a little walk up the heavily trafficked Cross Bay Boulevard. But you feel a world away. Not at all like you're in New York City.

      For one, Broad Channel -- http://www.forgotten-n... -- the community you'll walk through to the refuge -- is a little piece of antiquarian New England. Sea-side town, for sure.

      Secondly, the refuge itself. My wife and I walked the trail around the West Pond -- the East Pond trail is overgrown and muddy right now -- and saw all sorts of birds, including a troop of five geese who marched across the path in single file. The terrapin beach is closed to the public because it's nesting season, but next time I go back, I'll check out that part, as well.

      You can see the city's skyline to the north and west, and there is some plane traffic given the proximity to JFK, but in general, you're isolated, and it's quiet and natural and beautiful.

      And rare. Thank you, New York City, for preserving this salt marsh. Thank you for providing this haven for birds on the Pacific flyway. Thank you for making it walking distance to public transportation. And thank you for offering such a nice visitor's center, as well.

      If you've never been, go. If you've already been, go again.

      3/19/2006 5 photos

      A delightful day trip for New Yorkers, easily accessible by car from Queens, Brooklyn and Manhattan.  This major refuge encompasses 9,155 acres of diverse habitats, including salt marsh, upland field and woods, several fresh and brackish water ponds, and an open expanse of bay and islands. It is internationally renowned as a prime birding spot where thousands of water, land and shorebirds stop during migration.

      Over the years the refuge has been managed carefully to support a large variety of animals, and new animals and plant life have also been introduced.
