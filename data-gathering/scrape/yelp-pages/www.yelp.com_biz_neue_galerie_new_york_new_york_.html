      Review from Yael M.

       

      18friends

      48reviews

      Yael M.

      New York, NY

      2/7/2011

      I'm constantly amazed by the selection of museums in this city. It's easily to get overwhelmed in the Metropolitan Museum, and though I love it there, I've been making a conscious effort to get to know all of the smaller, more specialized institutions scattered across a relatively small patch of land.

      The Neue Galerie is one of those museums that I've walked by several times and didn't even know how to pronounce it correctly (it's German so "noy-yah" instead of "new" as I was saying). After being alerted to the museum's First Fridays program, when the museum opens up to the public for free from 6-8 p.m. on the first Friday of every month, I decided to give it a go and start my weekend off with some culture.

      Another awesome thing about smaller art museums in the city is that they all seem to inhabit remarkably interesting buildings. The Neue Galerie is housed in a Beaux-Arts style mansion built in 1914 and last inhabited by Grace Wilson Vanderbilt in the 1950s. After serving as the offices of the YIVO Institute for Jewish Research, it was purchased by Serge Sabarsky, an art dealer, and Ronald Lauder, of Estee Lauder, in 1994 and restored to its original state. Celebrating its ten year anniversary this year, the museum opened as a center for German and Austrian art in 2001. Walking into the building, I like to imagine myself living in a mansion like that and what it must have been like. The architects who restored the Neue Galerie took great pains to keep everything original, even converting the dining room into a fully-functioning restaurant, Cafe Sabarsky, that packed quite the crowd on Friday night.

      Sadly, after arriving and checking my coat, I learned that the museum was in installation and only had two rooms open for viewing of their permanent collection. However, I thankfully arrived right when a curator-led tour began and I learned a lot of interesting factoids. The main artists featured are from the early 1900's Germany and Austria -- including Egon Schiele, Gustav Klimpt and Oskar Kokoschka. Schiele has this thing for bony fingers which is quite disturbing, but did some really interesting landscapes. Klimpt's infamous painting Portrait of Adele Bloch-Bauer (infamous because it was stolen by the Nazi's, restituted to it's proper heirs, but then sold to the museum in 2006), is beautiful and on display with sculptures it was presented alongside with a hundred years ago. The gallery also displays three gorgeous clocks made with precious materials and intricate decoration.

      After leaving the museum, my friends and I decided to make it a theme night and had dinner at Heidelberg on 2nd Avenue. Between the bratwurst, sauerkraut, spaetzle and mug of beer, dinner was delicious and a definite new experience. I would highly recommend combining the two activities for a date-night or casual friends outing.

      The schedule of First Fridays is on the Neue Galerie website. For March, the museum will be fully open and attendees will get to tour the latest exhibition Birth of the Modern: Style and Identity in Vienna 1900. I plan to go back and see what the rest of the museum has to offer.

      Susan L.

      3/7/2010

      We visited the Neue Galerie a few years ago and were not disappointed, although I understand why some people have commented on the coldness of the atmosphere.  I'd followed the museum's origins from its inception and I feel it was a true "labor of love" on the part of (the late) Serge Sabarsky and - particularly - Ronald S. Lauder.  The mansion housing the museum has a lovely interior and it's quite appropriate for the collection.  I've long been interested in the type of art represented at the Neue Galerie (particularly that of Gustav Klimt), having visited various museums in Vienna and Germany several times.  It was also rather thrilling to see the painting "Adele Bloch-Bauer", having read a lot about the painting's provenance.

      In terms of the steep admission fee ($15, except for students and seniors), I agree - and particularly when the Metropolitan Museum's admission is "only" $20 (also quite pricey - but you could easily spend an entire day at the Met, whereas that's definitely not the case at the Neue Galerie).  However; patronage of the Neue Galerie is obviously much more restrictive - and at the same time, it's not the type of museum you'd visit very often.  I think $10 would be more reasonable, but we felt it was certainly worth $15 to go there once.  Also note that there's free admission from 6-8 p.m. on the first Friday of every month (thank you, Mayor Bloomberg).

      Review from Lindsay K.

       

      7friends

      193reviews

      Lindsay K.

      Putnam County, NY

      5/18/2009

      I was just here with a friend of mine.  It was a rainy and cold April afternoon in New York, and our original destination was the Met.  But unfortunately for us, there was a massive line waiting to get into the Met.  It went down the stairs and up the sidewalk towards Central Park.  The long wait may have had something to do with the rain - I think they were making everyone check their umbrellas or something - but neither my friend nor I were inclined to spend any time waiting outside, so she suggested the nearby Neue Galerie.  Her sister had once worked there and my friend had visited the museum already, and as we were both eager to escape the rain, it seemed like a good choice.

      I didn't really know what to expect as I entered Neue Galerie.  It's a museum devoted exclusively to German and Austrian art of the early 20th century.  I will admit that I don't know much about art from these countries, and in passing the Neue Galerie on my trips to the Met, I'd noticed that it sometimes had weird exhibits, so I was never tempted to stop in myself.

      The museum is housed in a gorgeous old mansion that has been maintained with exquisite care.  The staff is professional and squared away, almost to the point of being rigid.  Security personnel and staff are everywhere.  Upon entering the museum, you are immediately ushered to one of two lines - the one for the admissions desk, where you pay to enter the museum itself, or the line for the popular Cafe Sabarsky, which is apparently modeled on Viennese cafes.  

      It costs $15 to tour the Neue Galerie - $12 if you are a student or a senior.   These fees border on the obnoxious, particularly since you only have to pay $10 to enter the Met.  Also, the Neue Galerie is not a large, grand space, and although they have an elevator system, you will most likely be using the mansion's ornate, winding staircase to make your way from floor to floor.  Throw in the small hallways between galleries and you've got some cramped space for yourself.

      It's also worth noting that children under 12 are not allowed at this museum, and anyone under 16 has to be accompanied by an adult. This initially might seem snobbish and unfair, but Neue Galerie isn't a child-friendly museum. Everything from the space to some of the artwork reflects this. A lot of the artwork, such as furniture, is not roped off - basically, the staff trusts you not to touch it, and they will descend like hawks if they think you're getting too close to something - but such lack of restriction might prove too tempting to frolicking kids, hence the age requirement. Besides, the ultra-serious staff probably wouldn't even let anyone with a Bugaboo stroller through the door - yes, I'm looking at you, Manhattan-Mom-Who-Brought-Her-Screaming-Baby-To-A- Showing-Of-The-Dark-Knight - so save yourself some grief and don't even attempt it.

      Anyway, it was raining when my friend and I arrived at Neue, and there was a guy at the front door whose sole purpose that afternoon was to assist patrons in placing their umbrellas in plastic holders.  He was nice and had things moving along far more quickly than the standstill that was happening outside the Met.  Once inside, we discovered that the place was an absolute zoo.  After paying our $15, my friend and I headed up the beautiful winding marble staircase to the second floor.

      I have to admit that this museum pleasantly surprised me.  The artwork from the permanent collection is interesting and noteworthy, and Gustav Klimt fans will go nuts in this place, as they have several of his paintings on display.  The lovely Klimt painting "Adele Bloch-Bauer I" is on display on the second floor, and the bright golden shimmer of this beautiful work is amazing.  There's always a crowd of people around this painting too, hovering like paparazzi.  I'm sure Adele doesn't mind, though.  She's been quietly glamorous since 1905.  Lindsay Lohan and her ilk can take a lesson from Adele.

      There's lots of other great works of art here, and the display cases featuring jewelry, dishes and flatware, some of it constructed during World Wars I and II, are interesting to observe.  My friend and I also hit up the Brücke exhibit, which is currently running.  This was great as well - I'd never heard of this art movement prior to my visit - and it featured paintings, sculptures, prints and woodcuts completed by the artists involved in this movement.  It was a small exhibit, but very detailed.  And the German music playing softly in one room was a nice touch.  It kind of made you feel like you were in Vienna or Berlin during the 1930's, in some kind of calm before the storm, just prior to the Nazis bearing down on everyone and everything.

      All in all, this was a really nice gallery and I'm so glad I had the chance to visit.  While the entrance fee is annoying and the gift shop/membership fees are ludicrously expensive, the Neue Galerie is worth a visit.  So skip the Met.  See Neue.

      Review from Sooj O.

       

      148friends

      363reviews

      Sooj O.

      New York, NY

      2/27/2007

      A small museum that only displays art and artworks from Germany and Austria from the first few decades of the twentieth century, the Neue Galerie has become one of my favorite museums.  It is small enough to meander through in one afternoon while the collection is interesting enough for me to come back and always be intriguied.

      Some of their strongest holdings are in furniture and craft objects.  I love the stuff they have from the Wiener Werkstatte movement as well as the Bauhaus silverware.  They have a particularly good Josef Hoffmann collection...it's amazing to look at the clean contemporary lines and realize that they were made at the turn of the century.

      Other noteworthy items are the Gustav Klimt, Egon Schiele and Otto Dix pieces.  They also have Klee, Kandinsky and Grosz.  I want to go back and see an Oscar Kokoschka that was pointed out to me a couple of years ago.

      I like that their exhibitions tend to be of artists or movements of which I know very little.  

      Their main collection comes from the private collections of two people: Ronald Lauder (of the Estee Lauder family) and Serge Sabarsky.  The building belongs to the Lauder family and was completely renovated for the museum.  While many museums built from private collections tend to lack focus (the Getty particularly comes to my mind), the Neue Galerie has an advantage in that the two collectors were fascinated by only the early Viennese and Germany art.

      If you haven't been there yet, you are in for a treat.  Oh, if there's a major exhibition at the Neue Galerie, it can be a bit of a headache as the place is really quite small.

      Klimt's "Adele Bloch-Bauer I" caught my eye across a crowded room, she was just so... shiny!  All the news photos tend to emphasize her face, which is full of (lusty?) attitude.  But what really struck me were the luminous golden background and her intricate dress. Reproductions don't do it justice.  I wouldn't pay $135 million for it, mind you, but I can see why it fetched such a price.

      I also enjoyed the Van Gogh and Expressionism exhibit. As my friend said, Who doesn't like Van Gogh? A bunch of nutty German artists in 1911, that's who. The wall text said that when the Kunsthalle Bremen purchased Van Gogh's work, these artists signed a petition denouncing the foreign influence on the German art scene. That Germany had some nationalism issues, huh. Aaanyway, the exhibit showed Van Gogh's influence on other less xenophobic artists, and it was great.  The colorful Kandinsky and Klimt's beautiful pond & orchard were the highlights for me.  And of course the Van Goghs themselves were riveting -- You really had to see the brushwork up close to appreciate these masterpieces. Unless you plan to hit the Netherlands soon, take the opportunity to see these works stateside.

      Only problem with this museum is that admission is steep: $15, and for a very small exhibit.  It's hard to make time for this place when you've got that pay-what-you-wish Metropolitan behemoth down the street.

      My friend raves about the desserts here. Will be back to try them.

      Review from Sienna K.

      Sienna K.

      Philadelphia, PA

      1/27/2008

      I'm really lost as to how to grade the Neue Galerie. While the three stars I gave do no justice to the impressive collection and the admirable effort to encourage public interest in German and Austrian art, giving four stars would also seem to suggest that I left the museum impressed or provoked, when in actuality I've left every single time rather been unsatisfied and apathetic.

      Someone had written that the Neue Galerie has the focus that many other museums built from private collections lack; I have to disagree, for while the collection is obviously anchored in Austrian and German art, there seems to be no conscious effort to discuss what is Austrian and German art; random pieces from the same time and place doesn't really denote focus.

      And while the museum gives the obvious background of movements and artists, I never felt satisfied with how the works engaged the social and intellectual movements that I always found made German art so compelling. And with special exhibitions, it almost feels as though the museum felt so desperate to fill the space, that they included a lot of "fluff"-- in addition to the works and sketches of the artists were a lot of things that only seemed remotely related to the artist, especially without the given context.

      The size of the museum makes it perfect for one afternoon stroll. While there are some really amazing works from a time and place that we tend to forget a lot of in art, there weren't (for me at least) any piece that made me want to study it for an extended period of time.

      The decorative arts features are rather interesting, but again, very little context behind it. I also felt that with the Klimt exhibition, they could have done a better job stressing how important decorative arts was to the artist, but it was mentioned only in passing.

      The current Klimt exhibition (apparently the first major one in the US) seemed a bit too much. I'm wondering if the museum could have done a better job if it only devoted one floor, and thus edited out some things presented. As someone else mentioned, there are very few paintings (eight total); though they are from divergent periods, and remind you that Klimt's works weren't always like Byzantine mosaics, it just seemed like a sample collection-- meaning there was no thought in presenting them as a group. I actually enjoyed the sketches a lot, especially as they challenged my preconceptions of Klimt (whom I unfairly and wrongly had considered a bit too Hallmark).

      Review from Chris C.

       

      1friend

      24reviews

      Chris C.

      Farmingville, NY

      9/2/2008

      What makes the Neue Galerie fascinating is that it has no qualms in not fully explaining its own relevance or why the works of art contained are important for "you" and "your understanding."  It just is.

      Attending the Neue Galerie necessitates - though not fully requires - an interest in the collective art movements that would come to be known as "Modernism."  It will display some important pieces of work as reference points; however, it is up to the individual to be able to draw connections based on their knowledge and understanding of German-Austrian art at the turn of the century.

      What makes the Neue Galerie successful in its execution is its strong attempt to reflect the art presented.  It does not tell you its relevance or fashion it in a way that could be explained in a bland paragraph printed on a wall.  If it did that then it would not be art.  It would be another decadent space where disinterested tourists go and expect to be moved, and as a result disappointed, because they have a false understanding of the importance art has on our lives.  As inane as that might sound, and it sounds very inane, it's the truth.

      That's what makes the Neue Galerie a great museum: like the art it presents it resists pandering to expectations.  It appeals to a form of reflection that looks to detatch itself from bourgeoisie materialism and hone in on that which is more essential: life.

      I just said "bourgeoisie materialism."  What does that even mean?  (I think) It refers to the belief that by satisfying material desires we are able to reach a certain form of enlightenment or illumination.  This is counter-intuitive as no amount of "wealth" is able to give you an elevated understanding of the world at large.  The Neue Galerie actively avoids the aforementioned notion.  What you get instead is a display of the beginning of what we would come to know as modern art: art that meditates on medium and the facilities of cognitive processing.

      What it comes down to is the following: if you expect to be told why works of art are relevant then you will be sorely disappointed; however, if you go to the Neue Galerie simply desiring to enhance your perspective from an aesthetic and scholarly standpoint, you will be quietly pleased.
