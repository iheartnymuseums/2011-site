      Review from Brian G.

       

      4friends

      7reviews

      Brian G.

      Jackson Heights, NY

      2/1/2011

      Let me just start by saying that I love that there are only 14 reviews of this museum... its indicative of just how much of a hidden gem this place is. The Merchant House is one of those places that people from New York or have lived here for many years will go "I can't BELIEVE I didn't know about this place."

      I've been to the museum twice thus far. It's a unique experience to enter an NYC museum by ringing a doorbell and being greeted by an excited, warm, and (I imagine) unpaid employee. Something even more lovable and unique is the museum's lack of branded signage hanging outside. There's a single, weathered metal plaque denoting what this otherwise unassuming building is, and it's not unlikely that you pass it by the first time you go.

      I love the setup of the experience, which is to get a copy of your guide to the house (which is a nicely-sized volunteer-made binder, not a disposable fancy printed guide), which offers a history of its owners, how it came to become a museum, and a room-by-room account of life in 19th Century New York. You take in the house at your own pace, reading as you go. There are volunteers available to answer questions if you have them. I think there are guided tours as well, but the house is relatively small and its fun to sit in the rooms and read about them on your own anyway.

      The first time I went to the museum it was around Halloween and the house was decorated to mirror the way it would have looked during the death of one of its inhabitants. Candles, black drapery over mirrors, and even a full-sized effigy of a corpse made the experience even crazier. They were really creative with the experience as well: in one of the bedrooms they perfumed the air with flowers commonly used to mask the smell of the dead and played the sound of dripping water (like that of the ice used to slow decomposition leading up to funerary proceedings. There was a "special exhibition" of post-mortem photography (especially for infants and children, so parents could remember their too-young-to-die loved ones) that completed the morbid theme. Crazier still: one of the volunteers told me about an annual tradition the house has on Halloween where they enact a mock funeral (complete with period-dressed priest) for the effigy/dead family member, where people who buy tickets come in costume and then participate in carrying the effigy to the cemetery beside St. Marks on the Bowery Church, as they would have done in the 19th Century. WHat?! How crazy is that??

      The second time I went was around Christmas, and they had some decorations up that were used during that same time period, and supplemented the guide binders with some information about how Christmas traditions evolved over the years. The point I'm trying to make is that the museum is always doing something special that further gives patrons unique insight into the lives of 19th century New Yorkers.

      One recommendation I will make is to try to read up on any NYC history (esp.that time period) you cant before going, it only serves to enrich the experience for you. I had just finished reading Caleb Carr's The Alienist before my first visit and I was allowing the full force of my geekdom to take over. Enjoy!

      11/7/2010

      This house is a real gem, not only to New York City, but to the country as a fascinating example of how a 19th century American household would have looked. It is the only such house in NYC that has survived with almost all of its original 1830s furnishings intact and preserved. The self-guided tour is very informative and the docents who wander the halls are a wealth of knowledge about the Tredwell family and general life in 19th century New York. I learned a great deal while I was there today.

      Admission is only $10, $5 (seniors and students)

      Check the website for events going on at the museum as there are constant goings-on from mock 19th century funerals to photography exhibitions to plays.

      I highly recommend this time capsule of American history!

      The reviews below hinted at this but really didn't explain that the Merchant Museum is considered a paranormal hotspot--dating back to the 19th century, there have been many accounts of ghosts still occupying the house. As such, many of the programs in this tiny museum are dedicated to exploring the mysteries of Olde New York and the house itself. I recently attended a play they had here, "The Turn of the Screw" and it was fabulous--performed in a large parlor by candlelight--very enchanting and even if you don't believe in ghosts, the atmospheric appeal is undeniable. Check out their programs for many affordable performances and educational events. Overall a place that every genuine New Yorker must visit. But it's not exactly extensive, so seen once is seen enough.

      Review from Heath R.

      Heath R.

      Los Angeles, CA

      9/10/2006

      This museum is a little-known site -- and sight -- to see downtown. My gf and I checked it out a month or so ago, and I'm glad we did -- it's a great example of what a single-family home was like in the early- and mid-19th century.

      When we arrived, a guided tour was underway, and we were able to join midway. The docent was kind enough to loop back to the beginning so we could benefit from the whole walk through. They're doing some work on one of the chandeliers, but everything else is original furnishings, and very few improvements have been made.

      The museum is notable for two reasons. One, when it first became a museum, it was an informal museum run by a family member who wasn't trained in museum design or preservation. So some alterations were made that aren't truly in line with what a professional would have done. Two, the gardener, our first impression of the place, is a wonderful man. He's friendly, well informed about the property, and given the amount of time he's been working there, truly and deeply cares about the place.

      I'd go back just to talk to him and hang out in the garden.

      Go. Go!

    About This Business

    Provided by business

    Specialties

    come discover what life was really like in mid-19th century new york city. the merchant's house museum is new york city's only family home preserved intact -- both inside and out -- from the 19th centuy. three floors with six period rooms display original furnishings, decorative arts, and more from the wealthy merchant family who once lived here. special events and exhibitions throughout the year highlight aspects of domestic life in nyc in the 1850s. visit merchantshouse.org for more information and to take a virtual 3-d tour of the house.

    History

    Established in 1936

    Built in 1832, the Merchant's House was home to the Tredwell family for 98 years, from 1835 to 1933 when the last daughter, Gertrude, died at the age of 93. The House, complete with original furnishings, decorative arts, and other family belongings, was opened to the public as a museum in 1936.
