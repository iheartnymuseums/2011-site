      When smushed into the far back corner of an overheated 6 train, nose way too close to something, let's just say, overripe, and having to stand on my tip toes to reach the ceiling pole, I often wish I was a master of escape. If you have ever been intrigued by the real master of escape, Harry Houdini, I would highly recommend a visit to the Jewish Museum before the exhibit Houdini: Art and Magic disappears.

      In this well-organized compendium of photos, video, diaries and magical contraptions (including THE Chinese Water Torture Cell and THE straightjacket), you will learn more about the Hungarian-born boy turned Wisconsinite locksmith apprentice, turned worldwide magical wonder than you ever wanted to know. You will see images from perhaps one of the most disturbing tricks around, which involves swallowing needles and thread, and then pulling the threaded needles back out. Yeah, it makes me cringe too. You will also have the opportunity for a photo op with the master himself.

      Head over to the museum on Saturdays for free admission. While you're there, be sure to check out the other exhibits, including the display of menorahs from the museum's permanent collection.

      Rachel K.

      New York, NY

      12/20/2010

      Let me start off by saying that the Jewish museum is free on Saturdays, which is a wonderful benefit. Most museums have a free night, but it's usually a weekday evening or some other time when there aren't many crowds. Being free an entire weekend day is such a boon (although of course not to observant Jews).

      I visited the Museum for the Houdini exhibit in December 2010, which was very well-curated. Houdini's diary, never before displayed publicly, was a particular highlight, even though I could barely read it (after more than 100 years, the writing's a bit faded). Just seeing this artificact recording his early years was exciting. As famous as Houdini is to us today, he started off in vaudeville, touring relentless, spending coutnelss hours practicing his exciting escapes. The exhibit really highlighted the progress of Houdini and the hard work he put into transforming himself from teenage travelling performer to the most accomplished and famous magician in the world. Seeing his original milk can, the trunk, and the video footage of his escapes, was quite thrilling.

      I also liked the way they mixed in modern art pieces about Houdini with the original artifacts. Some were a bit odd, to be sure, but most were an interesting reflection on the fact that, nearly a century later and we still revere Houdini as the penultimate master of his craft.

      I didn't see the rest of the museum, but would like to return at a later point to check out all the other collections.

      Security is pretty tight here - for example, you must either check your coat or wear it, you cannot carry your coat, which is a little odd considering that every visitor passes through a metal detector. However it's not unusual - the Morgan Library also has the same rule that you can't carry your coat, so I assume it is standard security measure. The guards were polite and courteous to our large group (nearly 30 people), and so I would rate Museum very highly and look forward to a return visit.

      Review from angela n.

      Elite '11

      343friends

      698reviews

      angela n.

      Washington, DC

      11/29/2006

      The Jewish Museum is free on Saturdays!
      http://www.thejewishmu...

      I'm not familiar with their permanent collection, but the special exhibits I've seen have been terrific, including the Modigliani show a while back and "Superheroes" and "Masters of American Comics."

      "Superheroes" highlighted several Jewish comic artists, including Joe Shuster and Jerry Siegel, who invented Superman.  The blurbs suggested that Superman was like a Moses figure because his parents sent him away on a spaceship (like Moses's parents sent him down the river), and that being adopted by the Kents was "the ultimate assimilation fantasy."  It was also interesting to see how the 1940s superhero plots reflected the real battles between good and evil during WWII (not to mention that so many superhero characters were orphans while Jewish families were being torn apart in real life). I'm not Jewish and I'd never really thought of how the authors' being Jewish could influence the comics, so this show was a revelation to me.  The show was curated by Jerry Robinson, who worked on  Batman and who created the Joker.  

      "Masters of American Comics" was even more superb.  Jack Kirby, especially, could masterfully evoke movement and strength in his Fantastic Four comics; he could make a static image look like a movie almost.   And the show emphasized that comics aren't just for kids: some of the content could be very adult and shocking. For example,  Harvey Kurtzman's "Two-Fisted Tales" war comics depicted the drowning of an enemy soldier and corpses floating down the stream.  Gary Panter's work had a horrifying post-nuclear holocaust plotline where the 'hero' saw a suffering horse and tried to decapitate it -- It was  panel after bloody panel of this.  R. Crumb's intense and disturbing work reflected some of society's most misogynist and racist elements.  All really interesting stuff, but definitely not for little kids!

      Gourmet G.

      San Francisco, CA

      8/1/2005 1 photo

      Years ago, an ad campaign for rye bread promised, 'You don't have to be Jewish to love Levy's.' The same holds true for this museum. Located in a French Gothic mansion along Fifth Avenue's Museum Mile, the institution's permanent collection includes more than 28,000 objects, among them paintings and sculpture, photographs, archaeological artifacts, numismatics, and ceremonial objects. Its core exhibition, Culture and Continuity: The Jewish Journey, offers a visual narrative spanning 4,000 years, an exploration of the essence of Jewish identity and Judaism's survival from ancient times to the present. The museum also serves the community with a wide range of programs, lectures, theatrical performances, film screenings, tours, readings, and workshops. The National Jewish Archive of Broadcasting is also housed here, with selections ranging from Sid Caesar's 'Your Show of Shows' to 'Seinfeld.'
