      Review from John W.

      Elite '11

      82friends

      132reviews

      John W.

      Queens, NY

      10/5/2010 3 photos

      I've been walking past this place for years, and for some reason never got up the urge to go inside.  Contemporary art exhibits always seem hit-or-miss, and in these budget-conscious times, if you're gonna spend movie money and end up feeling you'd have been better served just seeing a movie, then the place was a definite letdown.

      Last Sunday I lucked out.  My first visit was a few days before a few exhibition openings.  Three of the five gallery floors were closed for installation, so admission for the day was only $6.  The Brion Gysin show on the 2nd floor (which just ended its run, sorry) was one of the best single-artist showcases I've ever seen!  I like modern and contemporary art, it suits my mindset, however I'm far from an afficionado.  I'd never heard of Gysin before a few days ago, and I just adored the experience of soaking in this neglected genius' oeuvre.  

      The floors are spacious, so that allows for real depth of presentation.  I spent the better chunk of 3 hours on the Gysin exhibit alone.  

      There's an open-air patio open on weekends, offering views of the surrounding neighborhoods and a serious sense of vertigo on windy days.  :)  

      I had such a great time there, and that was only seeing a third of the museum.  I can't wait to go back to see the other floors.

      The New Museum looks like haphazard stack of gray boxes piled on top of one another. There is a sense of doom and gloom about the place, like a loft building gone wrong because they forgot to put in the windows. Its saving grace is the "Hell, Yes!" sign on the front, which caught me off guard when I laughed out loud.

      We took the elevator to the top, and started floor by floor from there. The top floor had an exhibit exploring modern Cairo, with films, trinkets and precisely drawn walking tours that try to give you a sense of being in the city. It was a fun exhibit, but I found it to be a bit amateur and never really got the sense of what feeling the exhibit was trying to give. If the exhibit were an essay, there would be no thesis.

      The rest of the floors were dedicated to their new exhibit, After Nature, which was a truly deflating experience. Usually the expression of futility in art buoys me up (call me crazy). In this case, the headless taxidermied horse, the limbless trees and the boarded up house made me feel like sinking into a little pile of hopelessness.

      Review from Tori K.

       

      207friends

      377reviews

      Tori K.

      New York, NY

      12/6/2007 1 photo

      The New Museum's mission is to promote "new art" and "new ideas."   It is the only museum in NY exclusively devoted to contemporary art.  TNM was founded by curator Marcia Tucker 30+ years ago in a one room office on Hudson street.  She moved it to a gallery space in the New School later that year, then Soho in 1983.  Finally in 2007 the museum has it's first freestanding building. The 7story building, a sculptural stack of rectilinear boxes shifted off axis designed by architects Sejima and Nishizawa/SANAA is itself a work of art;  a refreshing addition to the post Bowery Bum laden flop house...burgeoning luxury condo neighborhood.

      New ideas?  Well you will have to be the judge!  We are all in different phases and stages of our lives. What is new to you may be old hat to another.  Art is after all subjective.  I  enjoyed the white glossy library-educational center on the fifth floor.  The museum also has a top-floor sky room for private events; an ample182-seat theatre for performances and events; and a cafe on the ground floor.

      Addendum...
      I must state that I was distracted more than a few times by 3 separate young, but fully  grown women- who should know better -in real fur coats.  Wait...was I on the UES?  One was sporting a full length-what looked like Chinchilla. I was perturbed to say the least.  Every time she approached a piece I was looking at, I rolled my eyes at her, yet bit my tongue ( Tori K. has a hard time with restraint)  and squirmed away in utter disgust.  I even took the stairs to another floor to make a quick escape from rat girl... alas her  Siberian twin in a 3/4 version was there to torment me.

      These fur bearing sub-beings tarnished my experience.  To be honest I was so affected by their darkness, and  the internal screaming of  animals skinned alive for their vanity, that I could hardly focus on the art.  I am not one for throwing paint,  thus I will wait until it is warmer before I make a return visit.  

      I went last night -Thursday.  Every Thursday is free-ongoing.  WTF?...the fur bimbettes twins can shell out  the cash...work their dark magic on daddy...  for Chinchilla coats ...but they can't come up with a measly $12.oo  to endow the arts?  Tragic....but not to be confused with tragically hip.

      Cool building design, neat gallery, overrated sky-room

      NYC has so many museums, I don't think you can visit them all in a week, maybe even in a month. The New Museum is of the smaller category of museums that are cater to people that live in NYC rather than tourists. I have passed by too many times and still did not know what it is, so I decided to pay a visit this past weekend with a few friends.

      The ground floor is very modern looking (white everywhere), it almost looks like a lobby of an ultramodern hotel. After you pay the admissions ($12, pricey for museum, but not terrible), you are allowed access to their "monolithic" elevator that can probably fit a small elephant. Love the bright lime green paint in it.

      We first went to check out the sky room, which was, saidly, underwhelming. The city does look nice covered in white, but there really wasn't that much to see. We didn't try to go outside cuz of the freezing temperature. The drinks they serve in the sky room is from a lame looking street vendor cart. I didn't even bother to approach it.

      We then checked out the Elizabeth Peyton exhibit that is open til Jan 09. Her artwork is quite interesting, as she feminizes all her male subjects by adding bits of her own features to them, and then masculines all her female subjects (to who, I don't know). The most interesting paintings are of Al Gore and Michelle Obama. Her art work is very vibrant, full of life, and she captures her subjects in a very natural setting.

      Then we went down to the Mary Heilmann exhibit. The first word from my mouth is "What?" Although her art work is a lot bigger, the over abstraction makes it extremely hard to understand what she is trying to express, and didn't jive with the work from Peyton. Where as we spent almost 2 hrs in the EP exhibit, we barely spent 1/2 hr for Heilmann.

      Overall, I like this museum a lot. Its not crowded like MOMA, and you really get to appreciate the works of the artists, even if some is not as easy to understand as others.

      Three stars because I'm a big fan of SANAA, the firm that designed the building. The New Museum is SANAA's first big project in New York, and it looks great both inside and out. Yes, there are some confounding things, like the skylights that don't emit much light and the fact that the fire stair is the primary means of circulating unless you want to wait for the excruciatingly slow elevator. Somehow I still like the project. I also really like the bookstore, and many of the events that are held in the downstairs auditorium (esp. the "Get Weird" music series, which in the past has featured Grouper, one of my favorite musicians for the past year or two).

      But that's as much as I can say for the New Museum. I find their curatorial choices to be consistently, perplexingly bad. Remember their underwhelming and aptly named debut show, "Unmonumental"? Or the Elizabeth Peyton retrospective, which itself seemed to be some kind of meta-commentary on art world nepotism? Their most recent show of work by very young artists isn't much better, with a very few exceptions, in particular "Flywrench," the beautiful and crude op art-inspired video game by Mark Essen.

      I like hanging out on the roof deck with its funny snacks, but I'm not optimistic about seeing a lot of art here that I'll enjoy in the future. Even in its old location on Broadway, the New Museum's whole curatorial mission seemed to be focused on pissing me off. Oh well. It doesn't stop me from admiring the building every time it's within my line of sight.
