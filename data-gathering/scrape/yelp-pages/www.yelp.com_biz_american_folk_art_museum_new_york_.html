      As an avid art & museums lover to the bones, I made it my business to be at the grand opening of the American Folk Art Museum when it was opened in its current location at 45 W. 53rd Street, just near the Modern Museum of Art.

      The museum has about 5,000 artifacts in its collections dating back to 400 years of  American history ( many by Native Americans) of textiles, quilts, ceramic, drawings, maps, and works by 20- century artists and designers.

      Review from Madeline L.

       

      262friends

      149reviews

      Madeline L.

      Astoria, NY

      3/22/2009

      With each passing year, I seem to gain new perspective yet still retain my old ones.  I am myself when I was 3 yrs old, 7, 12, 18, or as a twenty something.  In that sense, I am looking forward to aging for I will acquire another lens.  Not necessarily an evolve lens, just a different one.     My younger self would have scoffed at folk art, and found it rather boring, unrefined, or homey.  My younger self wouldn't have been interested  in coming here, much less pay the full admission price.   I can see how visitors can view this museum as boring, unimpressive, or even a bit crass compared to high art.  And now I can also see how folk art is so cool.  

      In folk art, as in music, it is about the art, the piece but it also isn't.   It is also about the person behind the work, who like us wakes up in the morning, eat breakfast, and go about their everyday life.  For the artist this would include painting, quilting, or carving.  That is part of what this museum highlights - the folks that are behind the art, and the context of time the person was placed in.  The artist is a regular guy or embodies a certain humanity, just like you and just like me.  No, not all of it is done by grannies or puritan.  American Folk art represents the diversity in America and the history, time, change and the context of America.  

      The museum's permanent gallery highlighted four basic themes through the art:  symbolism, function, community, individualism.  I value multi-functionalism and resourcefulness.  I loved reading about how some of these artists started out doing their art with left over railroad tools from their job, and stones in their yard - hacked tools and scraps, but found a calling in their pursuit.  I also love that the folk art museum discuss community and individualism not as binaries, but how those concepts are related and how both community and individualism operates together.  

      I did not go here on the free Friday afternoon.  Yes *gasp*  I paid full price to get into this museum.  Although, I did tell the lady I have a student ID but wasn't carrying it on me.  She ignored that comment and charge me adult price.  Do I look like an adult to you!!?!? This is when my 5 yr/o self was about to come out and throw a temper tantrum at the lady.  But even so, I had such a pleasant afternoon going through the museum.  

      Yes - like other yelpers have mentioned, it is a small museum.  You can go through the whole thing in 2 hours.  But why is that considered a bad thing?  Folk art (although widely practice) hold a marginalized but sturdy place in American art.  So, how fitting for it to be small.  How fitting for it to be next to MOMA.  No, I don't see it as the little folk art museum having it tough, or being in competition with the MOMA.  For folk art can hold its own.   Folk art is not in competition with the MOMA, rather they are complimentary, and that is perhaps why they stand side by side.

      Listed in: Artsy Fartsy!

      I came on a Friday, when it's FREE from 530 - 730pm. This place is snug next to the MoMa, but come here! It's free on Fridays and not that crowded! There is also some excellent music during the free period as well.

      I have to admit it, I love folk art. I like the colors, the shapes and the lack of proper perspective (in some.) I like the collection they have, but I'm unsure as to why they have a copy of the Book of Hours? I mean, maybe that's the original folk art or something, but that goes back to the 1450s and is French. Anyway. The rest of the collection is appropriately timed and placed.

      The museum stretches up five floors and spirals around. There is a cafe on the second floor and bathrooms scattered around. For those unable to take stairs, or are just really lazy, there is also an elevator.

      There is also something for the kids on Friday as well. A group of them were discussing portraits, moods and why people didn't smile for paintings. It was cute, I think they got to draw their own portraits later, even. So take some kids, make them learn about art.

      This place sits next door to MOMA which makes for some stiff competition, but it's a fantastic museum that deserves more attention than it receives.

      The Henry Darger exhibition was frentic to say the least.  His work here includes a volume of the 15,000+ page "In the Realms of the Unreal," and dozens of illustrations and watercolors.  None of which was discovered until after his death.

      The permanent collection includes everything from weathervanes to cutlery and is a varied yet seemingly comprehensive collection of folk art.

      There are also two sweet staircases here.  The main staircase that is open and airy, and a tertiary staircase that sits alongside the wall and leads from the third to the fourth floor.

      Amazing.

      Come on everybody--what's my favorite word?  Say it with me: FREE.  Now included in my favorite category is Friday Night at the Folk Art Museum.  Nestled quietly and unobnoxiously next to the bright and crowded (and uber expensive) MoMA, this museum offers music and free admission on Friday nights from 5:30 to 7:30 PM.  This set up is perfect for this place in particular because it's small enough to see everything within that time period.   In fact, seeing how small it is, I wouldn't have wanted to pay admission to get through a museum with so little.

    History

    Established in 1961

    The American Folk Art Museum is the premier institution devoted to the aesthetic appreciation of traditional folk art and creative expressions of contemporary self-taught artists from the United States and abroad. The museum preserves, conserves, and interprets a comprehensive collection of the highest quality, with objects dating from the eighteenth century to the present. Its collection includes more than five thousand artworks spanning three centuries of American visual expression, from compelling portraits and dazzling quilts to powerful works by contemporary self-taught artists in a variety of mediums. The museum serves as an important source of information and scholarship in the field and is committed to making the study of folk art a vital part of the curriculum for school audiences.
