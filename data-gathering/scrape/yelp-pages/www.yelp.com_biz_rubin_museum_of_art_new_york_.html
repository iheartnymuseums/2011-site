      11/3/2010 1 Check-in Here

      This is a gorgeous building, with a beautiful spiral staircase winding through six floors of Asian art (Himalayan, for the most part). The entrance fee for an adult is $10, but available at the deep discount of $2 for a student, or someone who just happens to carry around a student ID for this express purpose.

      Indeed, it seems that the museum attracts a large number of students, judging by the apparent age of the attendees, as well as the lectures taking place on multiple floors. Each of these floors has a different theme, but I highly recommend checking out the Tibetan Shrine Room in the Gateway to Himalayan Art.

      The Rubin is dedicated to providing background and context for the art on display; everywhere you turn there are maps and explanations for what you are seeing, why, and where it came from. For certain paintings, there are interactive touchscreens which provide detailed information on whichever aspect of the piece you happen to point toward.

      I like it here.

      $2 student price-- amaaazing

      Pretty cool museum dedicated to Himalayan art. A spiral staircase runs up through the six floors of the museum, and I would recommend working your way up each floor to the top since it seems to make more sense that way. The second and third floors seem to be more of an introduction to Himalayan art and the religious aspects of it, while the floors above that are contemporary Himalayan art and special exhibits.

      I'm no Himalayan art buff at ALL, but I would say that the floors I enjoyed most were probably the fourth ("Tradition Transformed"-- a collection of contemporary Tibetan artists exploring personal/cultural/political issues) and the sixth ("Remember That You Will Die"-- kinda a cross-cultural look at death and dying in art). I was going to give this place 3 stars, but those two floors (especially the 4th) singlehandedly made me reconsider and add that extra star.

      Pretty interior, layout is good, and it wasn't crowded at all when I was there (a weekday mid-afternoon). Definitely a place you could enjoy if you're like me and know nothing really about art, but are willing to learn something new.

      Review from Matteo R.

      Elite '11

      283friends

      368reviews

      Matteo R.

      Brooklyn, NY

      11/9/2009

      Hands down one of the most beautiful museum experiences you can have in the city.

      This "little" (each floor seems small, but there are 6 of them!) museum focuses exclusively on art from the Himalayan region[1], with most objects dating from the 12th century onward. Before visiting the Rubin a few months ago for the first time, I never had the opportunity to visit such a large collection of Himalayan art, and I love the serene beauty of it!

      The museum itself is beautiful: the 6 floors revolve around a central steel-and-marble staircase that spirals dramatically towards the round glass "eye" at the top of the ceiling.

      The best time to visit the museum is on Friday, when the entrance is free from 7 to 10pm. Moreover, every Friday at 6pm, the café morphs into the K2 Lounge (two-for-one cocktails during the 6-7pm happy hour), with usually a DJ spinning live. They also have jazz performance, movies, and the free "TalkingStick" storytelling series (8:30pm) on the second Friday of each month.

      Surprisingly, the museum is not busy at all on Fridays: this makes a perfect date for some beautiful and unusual art (let's face it: not very many people can claim to be experts on Himalayan art), in a sleek and modern (and yet warm) setting, plus the opportunity for some nice cocktails at the cool K2 Lounge. I think your date will be impressed (I know I was)!

      It is also good for kids, since the "ExploreArt Galleries on the 3rd and 5th floors (with a video alcove on the 6th floor) take the viewer behind the scenes, answering questions about why and for whom the art was made".
      -------------------------------------------------- --------------------------------------
      [1] At the lower level they have photography exhibitions, and right now an exhibition about Carl Jung's "Red Book", which I strongly recommend even if you're not into psychoanalysis (although I do think you need a shrink, actually): I thoroughly enjoyed it and found it fascinating.

      From the Rubin Museum's web site: "The Red Book of C.G. Jung marks the first public presentation of what may be considered psychology's most influential unpublished work. Jung's fascination with mandalas--Tibetan Buddhist representations of the cosmos used to help reach enlightenment--is evident in these books where mandala structures figure prominently in many sketches and paintings."

      I've been hearing about the Rubin Museum of Art for a long time. They had exceptional exhibits, and apparently their Friday nights are not to be missed. I don't know why it took me so long to get here, it's one of those items on my to-do list that somehow kept getting de-prioritized. But this past lazy Saturday I finally did make it.

      I thought I'd start at the top floor, (the sixth) and work my way down. Figuring that I'd peruse / quickly browse as I do for most museums --- a couple extra minutes on special items, but all in 2 hours, two and half tops for six not-that-big floors. I underestimated, or had too much faith in my perusing abilities. I spent an hour on the sixth floor alone. I thought maybe it was me, that finally seeing artifacts embodying my beloved asanas (yogic poses), texts detailing the yogic traditions of people inspired me, naturally I would spend awhile here. But it wasn't just me. I noticed the sparse floor with people who "came around the same time", were equally fascinated after an hour. I couldn't even get my hardly-comes-to-class husband to leave. We were all captivated.

      The fifth floor with the Mandalas was equally hypnotic. It traced the progression of mandalas of tantric Buddhism. I ashamedly admit I didn't realize there was a "Tantric Buddhism" denomination. The mandalas were breathtaking. In my mind a mandala was a two-dimensional, circular, patterned image.There were mandalas in yoga studios, and they assisted with meditation. But I never really connected to them. Yet here in this space, I connected with each and every one of them. In one room, there were mandalas in every direction, surrounding a golden majestic Buddha, it felt magical. Later, I discovered the *three* dimensional mandala. Three! It was gold and it looked like it rose from my dreams of heaven.

      I didn't make it to the fourth floor and below. Our time was up. We had spent close to three hours just on the fifth and sixth floors, and the book store. I firmly believe that the energies of the objects affect the beholder, a mild kind of empathic connection. And in this museum each beholder is given a vision of peace, devotion and yearning. It wasn't so much an educational experience to be in the company of this art, as it was a mediation.

      Review from Jennifer N.

      Elite '11

      86friends

      489reviews

      Jennifer N.

      Washington, DC

      2/23/2010

      I should start by saying I adore small museums - Asia Society, Folk Art, MAD etc. - I love museums where I can actually see all of the exhibits in one visit without feeling that intense sense of museum fatigue. So I was primed to love the Rubin which, in a lot of ways, feels like Asia Society (especially with that enormous, sort of nonsensical center stair case that takes up a lot of actual space in the building).

      There is much to love about the Rubin - the collection is quite good.  While the Rubin organizes the collection into "exhibits" with each floor as the home for a different show, I found that other than the cosmos exhibit, there was really a lot of overlap between the exhibitions.  This isn't necessarily a problematic thing - it just means that going to see the Bardo show might feel a lot like seeing the "What is Himalayan Art?" show on the second floor.  All this to say, after about two hours, most of what I had seen had kind of blurred.  The only object that really stood out to me was a truly amazing multimedia presentation made by the Rubin in conjunction with the Natural History Museum - it was a visual representation of what we know about the solar system, other galaxies, etc.

      Two suggestions: first, the show on the fourth floor, about cosmology, detailed the ways that different religions - Jain, Hinduism, Buddhism- have created knowledge systems for understanding the universe.  The placards are really hard to understand if you don't have some training in understanding these religions (i.e. they use a lot of jargon).  I wanted to understand more about these religious systems as systems, but came away knowing little more.  

      Second, the cafe is adorable and serene and amazing but, without a doubt the most overpriced cafe I've ever seen -- $10.75 for *two* samosas? That's insane, even by NYC standards.

      This is one of the better small museums I've been to so far in New York.  Although it's a little deceptive to say that it's small, since Himalayan art and Hindu cosmology are such a black box for most Americans that the intellectual content of the museum is almost as vast as the larger museums in the city.  If you don't know a lot about Hinduism or Buddhism, it's going to be a heady - and probably somewhat confusing - visit.  But even then it would be well worth visiting because it presents an interesting, complicated and beautiful subject in a compelling building.  (To wit, the design of the place is meant to mirror the shapes used in Himalayan religions to represent the different aspects of the human personality.)

      The docents are also exceptionally informed and interesting.  Although not as scripted and polished as those at the Met, they're a wealth of information on the various subjects presented, and you often get the feeling that they're speaking about a subject important to their personal lives.  Additionally, each level has an alcove with books and computer presentations on the floor's exhibit, which help break down what you've seen so you can understand it better.  There's also a lot of placards and explanatory material scattered around the exhibits which give you useful context.

      I visited while the top floor contained an exhibit comparing Himalayan and Christian concepts of death and the afterlife, which was well-done and an interesting addition to the more permanent exhibits.

      If you're a college student with ID you get in for $2, which is great and balances out the pricey cafe a bit.  The gift and book store was good, having a mix of the random, pointless museum tchotchkes you'd expect and unique and funny gifts which actually might be worth buying.

      While the Rubin's main showcase is mostly Himalayan art dating as far back from the 12th century, it is definitely a contemporary and modern museum. I went to the museum on a Friday night to take advantage of the free admission and what I found at the Rubin was way more than my senses could handle.

      The Rubin was the first museum I have been to where it was loud. Loud from the K2 lounge, loud from the lecture and discussion, loud from the miscellaneous tours being given, and loud from the group of college girls chit chatting in the corner. I couldn't hear myself think. All I kept thinking was, "This is a museum. Shouldn't it be quiet? Where are the signs of a finger being held up to a mouth? Shhhhhh."

      There is plenty going on at the Rubin. It's a museum that welcomes debate, encourages discussion, exhibits art in the making, provokes feeling, etc. There is a theater in the basement, a bar on the first floor, and 5 floors of everything Buddha, Himalayan, Tibetan, Kathmandu and Nepalese. Art ranges from sculpture to scrolls to B/W photography. The Rubin also does an excellent job of explaining themes, history, and the background of specific works.

      My favorite piece at the Rubin was the Vajrasattva (The Lord of 100 Buddha Families) on the fourth floor. It's a deep red on cloth with Vajrasattva in the middle. If you look closer, you will see the many miniature Buddhas outlined in gold all stamped in a repetetive manner. Now I don't know the technique in how the piece was painted but it is very very neat with the details.

      I highly recommend the Rubin. It's a bit flashy but feels very rich. The building itself is an architectural dream. It was renovated from an old Barneys and is even undergoing expansion. The staircase in the middle is definitely a focal point. Think of the Rubin as a miniature Guggenheim. There are a few computer stations at the Rubin that allow you to browse the art online, learn about it and watch some films on the Himalayas and the Rubin. Definitely watch the one on the history of the Rubin. It's very informative, very cool (if you are into architecture), and very thought provoking. The 10 minute documentary is amazingly put together without feeling like propaganda and offers a very good message about art and the Rubin's well intentions.

      I loved the energy of the Rubin. I only wish it were a tad bit quieter so I could focus a little bit more on the art. But pack a bunch of intellectuals in a building all ready for hot discussion and what do you get? All you have to do is listen to find out. Whatever happened to quiet time at the museum? Like I said, the Rubin is a modern day museum. All rules out the door!

      After sipping our cocktails and perusing the museum store, we headed upstairs to the galleries, which are free to the public on Friday 7-10pm (note: you cannot take your drinks from the lounge into the museum - I don't think it would bode well for your karma to spill your martini on a Buddha). There are six floors in the museum, but after drinks it is ambitious enough to tackle one... As we wandered around looking slightly lost (none of us know very much about Tibetan/Himalayan art!), a very friendly tour guide offered to show us some of her favorite pieces and explain their significance. She showed us five iconic works and gave a brief but very insightful overview of where each one came from, what it represented and why it was created. This was a great background to understanding the rest of the pieces on the floor, and definitely allowed me to enjoy the pieces more thoroughly.

      The museum has a lot of great programs including free guided tours (with paid admission) and on-the-hour quick five-minute guides to the art which give you a good basis for understanding the works. I highly recommend the Friday nights - a great way to enjoy the early evening before heading out, and you score some cultural points and delicious drinks!
