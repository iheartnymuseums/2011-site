      Review from Sharon C.

      Elite '11

      9friends

      161reviews

      Sharon C.

      Brooklyn, NY

      1/23/2011 6 photos

      I've been to this museum twice, both times with kids, before the renovation, and after. The kids love the interactive exhibits in which you edit movie or TV scenes by swapping out different sound effects or music, and feeling the change it creates. You come to understand that video production is a huge collaboration of many talented individuals. Another fun thing is making animations. You can make a simple 10-frame Monty Python-like animation using paper cutouts and email it to yourself. The flip book activity is great too. 5 seconds of you in motion is recorded and converted into frames, the frames are printed and stapled together into a flip book that you can buy for $10 (kind of steep, I think).

      The gallery of antique TV sets is a great  conversation starter, especially between grandparents and grandkids. "Uncle George was first among his neighbors to have all the new technology, and he got one of those tiny TVs, and they set up folding chairs like a theater in the living room, and invited everyone in to watch Jack Benny..."

      Real Virtuality, running now until June, is appealing to both adults and children. It's art installations that involve architecture, painting, filmmaking, software engineering, etc. Some are just to be observed but some put the viewer in the scene, or change according to the viewer's point of view. The 3D glasses are plentiful, sturdy and CLEAN.

      Much as I enjoy taking my kids here, I'd like to come to this museum without them on a quiet weekday, so I can skip the interactive exhibits, read the walls more, and focus on more details. There's so much more to see and do, and a schedule of events, workshops and film showings. Check out their website.

      Review from Tom K.

      Elite '11

      38friends

      236reviews

      Tom K.

      Forest Hills, NY

      1/16/2011

      This recently renovated and reopened museum succeeded in informing, entertaining and provoking me during my visit. It also did something few places do-- It made me feel old.

      Why is that, you wonder? For my eyes came into contact with a display case that contained...a Nintendo Entertainment System. Nay, not just an NES but also a Sega Genesis and Atari VCS, all video game systems from my youth, now ensconced in protective plastic for today's youth of the world to marvel at like the Model T of previous generations.

      Realization of aging aside, I was pleasantly surprised to see such artifacts in the museum. Moving images aren't just movies and this museum offers a tour of images that have moved throughout history, from 19th century zoetropes to a prototype of the first video game to modern virtual reality.

      The ongoing exhibit is entitled Behind the Screen. You'll find much movie memorabilia here, such as telegrams from Orson Welles during the making of Citizen Kane, a Chewbacca mask, Robin Williams' Mrs. Doubtfire body suit, Star Wars and Star Trek toys (including a watch from 1993 that I actually owned and wore), soundtrack cover art (including sweet sweetback's badass song) and so much more.

      There are small set models for you to marvel over, collections of cameras, radios, tv's and sound equipment. You'll see one of the first home entertainment systems that looked like a piece of wooden furniture and had a small TV,record player and radio all built in. (Take that ipad!)

      As part of the grand reopening, there was a screening of Stanley Kubrick's 2001: A Space Odyssey. While there were a few technical difficulties with the screening (and these were expected based on a few words before the movie about ironing out some bugs), the sound and screen quality was superb. There's definitely potential here for watching the classics of the movie canon. (And classics of tomorrow).

      General admission is $10 and there was some confusion over coat check policy. Turns out you don't have to check our coat if you wear it but you can't carry your coat. And no flash photography!

      No food or drink in the movie theater. The cost of a ticket for 2001 was $15 which included admission. There's also a small gift shop that featured various books, dvds, t-shirts, pencils and ...well, what you'd expect in a gift shop.

      The overall feel of the space is modern, clean and spacious. Its actually reminiscent of 2001:A Space Odyssey's 1968 vision of what 2001 would look like, now realized in 2011. (Does that make it somehow retro then?)

      I can't wait to return for future events as the reopening of this museum is a plus not just for Astoria, Queens and the City but for cinephiles everywhere. It's an easy walk from the Steinway Street subway station and well worth the trip.

      To quote Al Jolson in the first talkie, "You ain't seen nothing yet".

The new theater is gorgeous, and the sound was pretty incredible.  They also had fancier 3D glasses than your typical movie theater.  I'm going to be checking their website to see when they have more screenings.  They also have great events where they have screenings of current films and have the actors/directors speak about them after.  

      One thing to be wary of-- this museum has a few exhibits that are definitely for children, and there is a high child to adult ration.  We had a child try to take over a machine we were using (thank you NYC parents for being so attentive... NOT!) so don't go if you're sensitive to too many kids.  I'll have to go back soon!

      There is something for everyone, young and old alike.
      The first floor has a gallery of temporary exhibits, a Museum of the Moving Image Store, a cafe, and the entrance to the Riklis Theater. The upper floors house the permanent exhibits. Classics and other interesting films are shown each weekend, and some weekdays, in the Rilkis Theater at the Museum of the Moving Image. Screenings are free with regular admission to the Museum. There is a little cafe in the Museum but the neighborhood offers more interesting and diverse  dining options.

      Children  love the display of "antique" home video consoles. I actually had many of the things displayed in the museum as a kid like "antique" televisions and so on. What does that say about my age?

      Everyone will enjoy looking at the nostalgic merchandising section where you can see those Star Trek and Star Wars dolls. I actually had a Beatles lunch box as a kid and my mom gave it to Goodwill. It was probably worth a fortune and would be in the Museum if it were around. You see things like that lunch box displayed here.

      Behind the Screen, the museum's core exhibition, is an innovative blend of more than 1,500 historical artifacts, commissioned art works, video clips and interactive exhibits that show how moving images are made, marketed and exhibited. Visitors are able to make animations, experiment with sound effects and create flipbooks of themselves, among other activities.Other rooms show the amazing digital imaging that is used in movies like King Kong to recreate NYC in the 40s, set design, special effects and other behind the scenes peaks. Of course, a favorite for all visitors is the line up of vintage video games. You can buy tokens for a quarter and play one of a dozen games including Tron, Defender, Frogger, Donkey Kong, Ms Pac Man, Asteroids and more. Like most museums in NYC classes are available. http://www.movingimage...
      Like most Museums in NYC you can hold events and birthday parties for your child. The museum is presently closed for renovation and will reopen Jan 15th 2011. Right now it is open by appointment only.

      3/4/2010

      While some of the CRT displays and interactive projects on the top floor feel a bit gimmicky and dated by 2010 standards, the eccentric collection on display here is enough to make the $5 suggested donation and 45 minutes of your time totally worth it. At first glance, this museum gives off the vibe of a children's museum with colorful large buttoned kiosks taking up the majority of the real-estate here...the fact that a lot of the pieces look pretty beat-up on top of that doesn't do much to eliminate that vibe either.

      In general, a lot of the interactivity offered in the displays feels a bit dated in the YouTube-era where practically anyone with a copy of iLife can replicate most of the stuff on display here without breaking much of a sweat, but I'm sure it would be a blast for the 14 and under set.

      Yet, the real gems lie when you really dig deep into their collection of memorabilia , the highlights include one of the Chewbacca masks worn during Star Wars and one of the miniatures used for establishing shots in Bladerunner. Likewise the 3D zoetrope at the entrance is really cool as well.

      But really, for me the real thing that pushes this museum over from a 3 to a 4 would be the fully functional arcade they have on the lower level. While I find it a bit depressing to see that arcades have become such a relic that you'd literally find one in a museum, they have a good selection of machines on display and they covered a lot of the history of games in a really fun way with their home consoles in the area too.

      Ultimately, I don't think this museum really has enough cool stuff to give a, adequate film geek any knowledge that they don't already know but at the same time, I had so much fun that I didn't really notice anyway.

      I was kind of iffy about going here at first, but there's actually something for everyone in this place. It's a small museum overall, but they make very good use of their wall and floor space. There are three floors overall:

      FIRST FLOOR:
      Gift shop and modern arcade (I say modern because they actually have an antique arcade upstairs). Katamari, DDR ripoffs, DK Jungle Beat, etc. Lots of kids hang out here, as you might expect.

      SECOND FLOOR:
      Here there's a focus on movies and shows - original props, collectible fan merchandise (like those old Star Trek figurines!), and some really cool clips you can listen to and watch. I *think* the little theater is on the second floor (I could be wrong), but when I visited they were showing a Flash Gordon marathon, which brought back so many awesome memories.

      THIRD FLOOR:
      This is the cool one. The whole floor is LOADED with amazing historical facts and information, a physical timeline of TVs (with samples of the tiny B&W single-channel ones up to the modern kind), fun interactive toys and gadgets, crazy old commercials and shorts playing everywhere, and a whole section in the corner full of old time arcade machines THAT YOU CAN ACTUALLY PLAY. All the old stuff from Pong and upwards. Some of the machines were down when I last went, but they probably keep good maintenance of them overall if there's people playing them all the time.

      Definitely worth checking out; you will most definitely learn something new. Bring your favorite TV enthusiast along and show them your Metrocard to get two-for-one admission.

    About This Business

    Provided by business

    Specialties

    Museum of the Moving Image advances the public understanding and appreciation of the art, history, technique, and technology of film, television, and digital media. It does so by collecting, preserving, and providing access to moving-image related artifacts; screening significant films and other moving-image works; presenting exhibitions of artifacts, artworks, and interactive experiences; and offering educational and interpretive programs to students, teachers, and the general public.

    History

    Established in 1988

    Museum of the Moving Image is located on the site of the Astoria Studio, one of the largest motion picture and television production facilities in the United States. Originally built as Paramount's East Coast production facility in 1920, over 120 silent and sound films were produced at the studio until it was taken over in 1942 by the U.S. Signal Corps and became known as the Army Pictorial Center. The building eventually fell into disuse, until a non-profit foundation restored it to productive use 1977. That year, "The Wiz" ushered in a new era of feature film production.

    In addition to reviving the studio, the foundation used one of the original studio buildings to establish a museum about motion pictures and television. Museum of the Moving Image opened to the public in 1988 and offers visitors an innovative blend of historical artifacts, commissioned art works, video clips, and interactive exhibits shows how moving images are made, marketed, and exhibited.
