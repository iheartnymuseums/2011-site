      Ami M.

      Turlock, CA

      5/19/2010 3 photos

      I hate it that all those years I lived in New York, I have never ever set foot in this museum.  It is so cool!  They probably have done renovations and updating because some parts and exhibits look modern, almost similar to the ones in the Science Center in Montreal.

      There are definitely plenty of hands-on exhibits here and just so much info that it is impossible to leave here without learning something new.  Unique to me here are the following (at least the ones I saw before the museum announced their closing):

      1. A simulation of the Alvin DSV (deep submergence vehicle)- not really a simulation, but more of a "pretend-you're-a-scientist-inside-the-Alvin" type of thing.  There were buttons and flip switches that made different sounds and commands. And it even had that claw-thing you maneuver to pick up pretend ocean rocks.  

      2. A scale to approximate the percentage of water in your body.  

      3. Several powerful microscopes with live microbes in them!  You can actually see them moving about-- flagella and all!   *body hair rises!*

      4. A non-computerized representation of the Bell Curve Distribution.  This will make you want to stand there all day and see how true the theory is!

      Sadly, during our brief visit, we did not even get to the other half of the museum.  We came during their spring hours so they close promptly at 2 pm.  But, apparently, while most museums close on Mondays, the NYHS is open (except Mondays in September thru March).  

      If you've got wee ones, there's a separate area just for them to enjoy and learn from also.  Parking is plentiful and relatively safe.

      Review from Cristina S.

       

      4friends

      Cristina S.

      Ridgewood, NY

      5/2/2010

      This place is pretty good for both parents and kids. Interesting for kids but not stressful for adults. The size of the place makes it an all day outing and helps keep it not too crowded. If you go in the dead winter or summer you want to be prepared with sunblock or mittens as a lot of exhibits take place out doors, including the miniature golf.

      The enclosed tots area is fairly large and well thought out. It has climbing challenges for crawlers and first walkers, lots of imaginative play toys (a kitchen, a mini supermarket, a garden, etc. They have a glorified train set from ikea and building blocks of various types.  An unpretentious place, the tots area doesn't try to give the impression that they are teaching rocket science to tots. Its just play and that's okay. The staff is young and friendly and actually helpful.  

      The second floor directly over the tot area has another enclosed area filled with touch-friendly manipulatives which is good for crawlers and those still unsteady on their feet. Its a good place to sit and rest under the skylights but can get pretty hot in the summer. The best  on this floor: The shadow exhibit. The lower level is best for older kids and adults, although our toddler seemed  to enjoy the water exhibits. The most impresive exhibit is the catapult exhibit outside behind the museum.

      Best bet is saturday mornings before 11 because its free and there is a small farmers' market outside. Bringing your own food is higly recommended as the cafeteria has very limited offerings. There is a parking lot but it is $10 which seems a lot when there is usually plenty of street side parking. The bathrooms are crowded and have no changing station on the first floor, best to use the second floor ones which are cleaner though still without a station.

      Overall really worth the trip and possibly even the membership if you make it there often enough, instead of play centers.

    About This Business

    Specialties

    The New York Hall of Science is New York City's hands-on science and technology center. Ranked one of the top science museums in the country, the Hall features more than 400 hands-on exhibits. Visit us and explore the wonder and excitement of biology, chemistry and physics.

    History

    Established in 1964

    The museum was established in 1964 as part of the World's Fair in Flushing Meadows-Corona Park and at the time was one of only a few science museums in existence. Unlike many other institutions, which were closed immediately or soon after the Fair, the Hall remained open after the fair, and served as a resource for students. The Hall remained open until 1979, when it closed for major renovations.

    Its re-opening in 1986 gave New York City a science museum for the first time since it had closed seven years earlier. The museum's growth and ability to draw crowds led to the city's pursuit of further funds and expansion.

    Additional expansions more than doubled the size of the museum, which today has more than 450 exhibits including the exhibitions that explore physics, biology, chemistry, and more. Through outstanding exhibitions and innovative education programs, the museum serves broad and diverse audiences.

    Meet the Manager: Margaret H.

    New York Hall of Science CEO & President Dr. Margaret Honey is widely recognized for her work using digital technologies to support children's learning across the disciplines of science, mathematics, engineering and technology.  Prior to joining the New York Hall of Science, she served as a Vice President of the Education Development Center and Director of EDC's Center for Children and Technology.
