      Continuing with my tour of Astoria, my friends took me here, to this hidden gem.  I've heard of Isamu Noguchi from landscape classes but he was an ultra talented man- landscape architect, sculptor, set designer, furniture designer...and so much more. I knew he did most of his work in the East Coast but it's across from the museum he designed himself in Astoria.

      First Fridays offers extended hours and pay as you wish.

      Learn to be still, my heart.  Go.

      Review from David P.

      Elite '11

      314friends

      David P.

      Astoria, NY

      7/25/2010

      This museum works on many levels.  

      Let's start with the obvious -- the art.  While I'm not a big fan of the Easter Island style sculptures, there is enough variation in the art to find something to connect with.  I particularly loved something that looked like a cross section of a conch shell, with stairs carved into it.  The stairs transition to a smooth slide.  Words don't do it justice, and damn it, I forgot my camera!  

      The museum also works as a place of zen and peace, although that peace was momentarily shattered by the screaming kids in the gift shop.  I don't think the museum sees much foot traffic, probably because it's a little out of the way.  I was able to wander the floors with only a few other visitors.  And, of course there is a lovely outdoor space.  On sweltering hot days, however, I prefer the air conditioning.  

      Lastly, the museum offers a bit of a history lesson.  I didn't know Noguchi from a hole in the ground.  They show a video biography that I thought was extremely informative without being dry.  Did you know he created those Japanese paper lantern lamps?  You can buy some in the gift shop if money is no object.  Otherwise, there are always IKEA knock-offs.

      Review from Renee C.

      4/28/2009

      I've been a fan of Isamu Noguchi's work for a while since I passed by his garden in the UNESCO headquarters in Paris, the Red Cube outside the HSBC building here and the Sunken Garden @ Yale. Now having visited his museum, I was even more intrigued.
      The building was architectually pleasing. Shaped like a flat iron, the exterior was brown stone and the interior was simple and modern. Don't miss the garden because that is he most important part of the museum. I enjoyed the more crafted pieces inside too.

      We are somewhat similar, Isamu Noguchi and I - we both questioned our backgrounds and wondered if one is more dominant than the other or whether there can be a balance out there that can be achieved. Just always in search of an identity, I suppose,....something that will never really end.

      I was very impressed to learn that he went to France early in his career and was an apprentice to Constantin Brancusi (one of my FAVORITE artists of all time!!) . He didn't speak French and Brancusi did not speak English, somehow they had an understanding.

      It took less than 45 mins to get here from Union Square, but that should not be a factor to sway you away from this sculpture museum.

      Review from Frank W.

       

      133friends

      302reviews

      Frank W.

      New York, NY

      1/5/2009

      The Isamu Noguchi Museum is a calming refuge, a chance to reflect on simplicity, and to contemplate the history of abstract art in the 20th Century.  The sculpture garden is normally one of the highlights, but is currently closed for renovation.

      There is a beauty and simplicity to Noguchi's art but a close look gives you an idea of how much the guy must have pounded away to get all of the different textures on his  sculptures and how he hard he must have worked to create some of his rounded and looping shapes.  Some pieces, look just like big beautiful rocks, but you can look more closely to see how they are displayed and altered to evoke certain feelings, ideas, or a sense that there is an orderly peace in the universe and in ourselves.

      For those who don't like abstract art this might not be the museum for you, or it might change your mind.  Some of the works here are representational, including some of his best.  The informative documentary that plays in the museum (on a faux wood-paneled TV!) implies that his portrait heads were a bit of a sellout, but seeing his style and sensitivity applied to human beings was amazing.  Just like his rocks, some appeared fragile, sensitive, and pock-marked.  Others were smooth and serene.  His friend the architect and futurist Buckminster Fuller was headstrong and shimmering in chrome plated bronze.

      Noguchi didn't just hang out with Fuller.  He was a collaborator with Martha Graham, found a patron in George Gershwin, and had an affair with Frieda Kahlo.  Despite the serenity of many of Noguchi's works and all of his success, it seems he was never completely comfortable in America.   He must have gotten great satisfaction and release pounding away on all those rocks, though, and the many visitors to the museum have benefited from the results

      I first discovered Noguchi in the post office of all places, buying the beautiful stamps that featured his works a few years ago. There was something so nice and perfect about them, I thought at the time. Now I marvel at how this man could make even a postage stamp and a building in Long Island City seem so transcendent.

      Sam L.

      Detroit, MI

      1/20/2008 2 photos

      The Noguchi Museum is a quiet little refuge located in an otherwise industrial section of Astoria near the East River.  Upon entering, it's immediately obvious that the museum's simple design and serene atmosphere are extensions of Noguchi's artistic sensibility.

      Now, I know nothing about abstract expressionist sculpture, but even I could appreciate the beauty of Noguchi's pieces.  The museum's spacious layout allows you to focus on one piece of art individually and without distraction.  If you look at the pieces from a distance,  the shapes appear simple and basic.  But when you get up close to them, the dense and textured rock and its rich designs and markings impart a deep, dense, and profound quality.  I uploaded two photos of "Sun at Noon" and "Emanation" (or as I like to call it, 'Stone Cantaloupe') as examples.  This dichotomy was really cool and gave me a deeper understanding of the artwork.  

      The outdoor garden is, of course, beautiful and serene.  Simply sitting on the benches and taking in the sculptures in the open air, I felt rejuvenated by the peaceful environment.  For the ten dollar admission fee, the museum visit was like a soothing massage for the soul.

    About This Business

    Provided by business

    Specialties

    Current Exhibition: November 17, 2010 - April 24, 2011

    On Becoming an Artist: Isamu Noguchi and His Contemporaries, 1922-1960 explores Noguchi's friendships with seminal figures including artists Constantin Brancusi and Frida Kahlo; designer and inventor Buckminster Fuller; architects including Gordon Bunshaft and Louis Kahn; and dancers and choreographers Martha Graham and Merce Cunningham. These relationships are brought to life through artwork and historic documentation, providing a glimpse into the art world during this time.

    Free Gallery Talks: Every Wed thru Sun at 2pm, we offer Educator-led gallery talks, open to all visitors. Reservations not required. Japanese tour also available on First Fridays and Second Sundays.

    Second Sundays: Events, e.g music performances, poetry readings, and panel discussions on the second Sunday of each month @ 3pm.
    For an updated list of upcoming performances and panel discussions, visit www.noguchi.org.
    All programs free with Museum admission.

    History

    Established in 1985

    The Noguchi Museum opened as the first museum in the country to be founded by an artist during his lifetime and dedicated to his work.  The collection and exhibitions focus on Isamu Noguchi's extensive production, articulating the cultural time in which he worked, the many, major cultural figures with whom he engaged, and his influence on the art and design of today.  

    The Noguchi Museum offers a variety of education and public programs that seek to introduce the work and vision of Isamu Noguchi to diverse audiences.  These programs encourage the investigation of Noguchi's work from different vantage points, and support participants as they experience the artist's work from their own perspectives.
