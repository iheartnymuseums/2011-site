      Review from Samantha S.

      Elite '11

      40friends

      199reviews

      Samantha S.

      New York, NY

      8/30/2010

      Yes, they've got fighter jets and helicopters and a submarine and a Concorde and a replica space capsule. Yes, there are simulators and interactive games (we failed at all of them, sinking any number of animated ships). Yes, this aircraft carrier is a massive feat of design,  engineering, and ingenuity.

      But the Intrepid is also a place where you'll see old men choking up during the movie that explains WWII kamikaze attacks and their effects--on both sides. It's just that moving. And it was a reminder that for all the STUFF on the Intrepid (see above), history is really about people, not things.

      Anyway, enough of the emoting.

      Things that are great about the Intrepid (aside from the obvious):
      * The 90-minute guided tour. You get to do things not available to the general public: ride the aircraft elevator, see a restoration in progress, and hear some amazing stories. What's a blood chit? Why did the deck used to be made of teak? What does a pink eraser have to do with aircraft design? Military history makes more sense with the tour guides explaining it.
      * The Growler submarine. The line looks long but moves reasonably quickly. Your studio apartment will seem spacious in comparison.
      * The movies. Really well done, with commentary by sailors and airmen of the period. One involved sirens and simulated smoke, which sounds hokey, but actually had the desired effect.
      * The spectacular panorama of the Hudson spread out in front of you.

      Things that are not so good:
      * The price. It's crazy. Wait for a special occasion, and take someone as a birthday present.
      * The exhibits of living quarters and mess hall. Boring.
      * The bridge, unless you're a sailor. I couldn't figure out what I was looking at, or how it was used.
      * The Concorde. Turns out, the interior looks just like a plane! Skip it.

      UPDATE: Once we got home, we realized that my husband had been the victim of a pickpocket here. Keep a close eye on your wallet when you visit.

      Review from James N.

       

      2friends

      25reviews

      James N.

      Queens, NY

      10/14/2009 7 photos

      I was so thrilled to have the USS Intrepid Sea, Air & Space Museum return from it's long duration away from it's pier.  Although I have no military background, I still happen to be a military enthusiast and have visited her about six times.

      The main attraction, the Intrepid, an Essex class aircraft carrier from WWII, comes back restored and repaired with a new coat of paint, some new aircraft on display, and more areas open for the public to view, one of which is the mess hall and kitchen.  It is amazing how the kitchen in an aircraft carrier is churning out so many meals 3 times a day everyday for a crew of over 2 thousand sailors and airmen!  The food are not cooked in pots but in huge kettles, and there must have been half a dozen large grills and large commercial ovens in that one section alone.

      There are interactive computer games where one can pretend to land a jet plane onto the deck of the carrier.  Also, there is a helicopter on display that one can climb into and have your photo taken by their staff.  At the stern of the ship, there are a bunch of flight simulators to enjoy, but are rather kind of costly.  There is also a cargo net climbing activity children can enjoy.

      On the flight deck, there is a good collection of aircraft from the US and from other countries such as the former Soviet Union, France, and the UK.  My favorite plane, the Grumman Corp. F-14 Tomcat is still on display.  From there, one can go up to the "Island" of the ship and sit on the Captain's chair and see the amazing view from the bridge.  In the hanger deck below, there are exhibits about space travel and a "Kamikaze" attack simulation show with lighting and smoke effects.

      The USS Growler submarine is also on display and is just as fun and interesting to tour.  I would recommend this as the first of your activity when beginning your museum tour.  Just be aware, there is a height and body dexterity requirement before entering the submarine.  There is usually a wait time anywhere from 10 to 30 minutes.  You will be amazed at how cramped the living conditions the sailors had to endure months at a time out in the sea.  From years back, I recall the tour guide had mentioned the submarines had limited capacity of storing and making fresh water, so showers were limited to only two to three minutes and given once every two weeks!

      At the end of the pier, is a British Airways SST Concorde, now all decommissioned.  At it's time of use, it was the only commercial passenger jet capable going super sonic.  The interior is surprisingly small and tight, makes one wonder how luxurious could it really be?

      From the other yelp reviews, I did not realized that Bank of America card holders could get in free during the first week of the month.  I was able to get a discount using my "Entertainment" book where one can purchase a ticket at full price and receive another ticket free which was fine as I went there with my wife.  I noticed the McDonald's has been replaced with an Au Bon Pain snack shop.

      This museum is great for anyone and a venue children would enjoy and have an educational experience in science and history at the same time.  It is worth the trip and there is the opportunity to meet with some of the Naval Veterans who have served on the Intrepid volunteering with great and deserved pride.

      Review from Matt E.

      Elite '11

      176friends

      1092reviews

      Matt E.

      Brooklyn, NY

      11/25/2009

      4.5 stars... If admission was closer to $10-12 instead of $22 ($18 for students and seniors) I'd give them 5 stars. They also have 3 different simulator rides for $8-10 ea (or $21 for all three)- $3-6 would also be more ideal.

      The place is awesome and includes the USS Intrepid aircraft carrier, a number of planes and helicopters (both foreign and domestic), as well as the USS Growler (submarine). They also have a Concord jet but that's really a waste of time checking out (at least the inside of it is). The sub is awesome to check out and you're brought through on a tour while the Intrepid is self guided and you can take as long as you want exploring all the levels of the ship. There are many workers (a few of which who were actually stationed on the ship) around to answer any and all of your questions which is awesome. Even if you're not a naval or aviation buff you'll still have a great time exploring the history of the place. The massive gift shop is also pretty cool too with something for everyone.

      As far as the history goes:

      The 872 foot Intrepid (aka The Fighting I) was built in 1941 in Virginia and was active from 1943-74. The crew was nearly 3,000 strong. It saw action in both WWII and the Vietnam War (amoung many other battles and missions). It helped sink 2 Japanese destroyers in 1944 and has been hit by 3 kamikaze fighter pilots. The Intrepid was also chosen to be the recovery ship for both the Mercury capsule (1962) and the Gemini 3 capsule (1965). In the 60s it was also equipped and designated an antisubmarine ship and spent many missions protecting the country from submarines. The Intrepid is also credited as being the first aircraft carrier to launch air-crafts with steam catapults (a feature they used to launch planes with only 26 seconds between take offs in the 1960s).

      The USS Growler was built in 1954 and was active from 1955-64. It's 318 feet long and is capable of holding and launching two Regulus missiles (or 4 Regulus II missiles)

      I'm one of those folks who are neutral/nonchalant about the Navy, Military, Army, etc. My dad taught me well, as he recounted tales for me about serving for the US Army in Korea. I have a lot of respect for those that pursue the armed forces, but it's not for me.

      That being said, one does not need to be a sea/air/space fanatic to enjoy the Intrepid. I went to the Intrepid three years ago right before it closed for renovations and again this past Saturday. The contrast is striking, and the repairs are well-done.

      The Mickey D's is no more, replaced with Au Bon Pain. Peeling paint is now refurbished. The heart of the Intrepid used to be a little dimmer, but is now full of light and contains more memorabilia. They now have these new theaters that are really something. It's not in a movie-theater style, but rather a triptych of movie screens and projectors with smoke for added effect. Totally epic.

      Highlights of the Intrepid include beautiful views, aircraft models, the sheer size of the ship itself, getting to see the Growler submarine, and sneaking a peek at the Concorde. There are some simulator rides one can take, with added fees, but there's more depth and fun to the Intrepid then when I went three years ago without having to pay the extra money.

      The admission fees are steep, but as stated previously, Bank of America cardholders get to go in free with one other person on the first weekend of the month. I also got a 10% discount card for two regular tickets when I went to the Intrepid for a free screening of "Jaws" on the deck in mid-July, which I used for my mom and sister to get into the museum this weekend. This is probably the only time I saw more males milling about a gift shop then females, which amused me.

      Definitely one of the things both NYC residents and tourists must visit at least once-- just not in the winter.

So EVERYTHING has to do with the presentation.

      The PROS:

      Seeing a Concorde up close, plus being able to walk inside, is extremely cool. It's a very impressive aircraft -- no wonder the Brits love the thing.

      The Blackbird a select few of the other aircraft are incredible. If you've never seen them before, they are very impressive.

      The U.S.S. Intrepid fought in WWII.

      CONS:

      Where to start? This place has really gone downhill. Back in the 1990s, I went to the Intrepid a number of times and it was a somewhat funky but very cool place -- filled with tons of fascinating exhibits and world-class models / dioramas. There was an entire portion of the Hanger Deck devoted to the Imperial Japanese Armed Forces and other aspects of WWII. How are you supposed to appreciate the Intrepid's history without this?

      The current displays are very spare and underwhelming. The couple of aircraft carrier models that they do have now are real clunkers with some lousy work.  There is also a dearth of WWII aircraft -- with the exception of the old standby dive bomber. The Space portion of the museum is near-nonexistent. There also used to be an awesome exhibit on the lower decks devoted to the Air War over Europe, which played old newsreels, a bombing scope...all kinds of memorabilia. Gone now.

      Having been to the Yasukuni Museum in Japan, I think we need to really shape up our act here in NYC. The Japanese have a tremendous display of their aircraft, ships, soldiers, pilots, and sailors. Not only that, but it presents the Pacific War in a totally flattering light to their "struggle" against the West. We here in the US deserve much better than this shallow, totally generic museum. Sure, the added some glass doors with the renovation, but the Intrepid Museum lost its soul.

    Specialties

    The mission of the Intrepid Sea, Air & Space Museum is to honor our heroes, educate the public and inspire our youth. As an educational and cultural not-for-profit institution centered on the aircraft carrier Intrepid, a National Historic Landmark, the Museum is dedicated to promoting the awareness and understanding of history, science and service through its collections, exhibitions and programming.

    History

    Established in 1982

    The Intrepid Sea, Air & Space Museum is one of America's leading historic, cultural and educational institutions.  Opened in 1982, the Museum has welcomed more than 10 million visitors.  The Museum is centered on the aircraft carrier Intrepid (CVS-11), one of the most successful ships in US history, and now a national historic landmark and one of the most unique attractions in New York City. In 1943, Intrepid was commissioned and served proudly in World War II.  She went on to serve as one of the primary recovery vessels for NASA, three tours of duty off Vietnam, and submarine surveillance in the North Atlantic during the Cold War. Today she continues her service as a premiere educational center and a monument to all who have served our nation in uniform.

    Since 1982, the Intrepid has become a national icon. More than 915,000 people visit each year.

    Meet the Business Owner: Intrepid Museum A.

    Intrepid Museum features a range of interactive exhibits and events providing a snapshot of heroism, education, and excitement.

    Intrepid  is dedicated to the exhibition and interpretation of history, science and service as related to its home aboard the USS Intrepid, a National Historic Landmark. As you explore the Museum you will be able to examine original artifacts, view historic video footage, and explore interactive exhibits. Visitors can also ride in the A-6 Cockpit Simulator, visit the Virtual Flight Zone, and tour the inside of the world's fastest commercial airplane, Concorde.
