      Review from Fatina A.

       

      11friends

      Fatina A.

      New York, NY

      2/15/2011

      The American Museum of Natural History is huge, comprising of some 25 interconnected buildings, and some 46 permanent exhibit halls, it contains over 32 million specimens! But of course only a minute fraction of those can be displayed at any given time, still, it is an impressive museum!

      My favorite part is the Hall of Human Biology and Evolution, which basically traces the entire evolution of the human race via some quite impressive exhibits and reconstructions. You get to learn about everything from the cognitive capabilities of our earliest ancestors, to viewing reconstructions of what they looked like, to castes of some of the most famous fossil evidence of our remote past. The dinosaur fossils are amazing as well!

      If you're interested in what you see in the museum and want to delve into the topics a bit further, then the research library consists of some 450,000 volumes, with some dating as far back as the 15th century.

      Admission is by donation only, so pay whatever you want or feel you can afford.

      I live right around the corner from the museum and so decided it made sense to become a member. I'm glad I did, because it's great to be able to randomly wander in whenever I feel like it, sometimes only for 45 minutes. Despite that, I still have never been in the museum when it's not unbelievably crowded - screaming, running kids and all. Be prepared. Bring your iPod.

      I'm from DC, so I'm definitely a museum snob. I know what well run places look like and I'm used to not paying for them, so impressing me is hard.

      Despite that, the AMNH is great, just in terms of sheer volume. I go all the time, yet I'm not 100% certain that I've actually seen everything. Sure, some things are a little retro and  not every hall is air-conditioned in the summer, but it's still fun to get lost and the collection is amazing.

Even if something sounds really interesting, if you're not a member, I would really think twice about shelling out an additional $15-$20, especially if you're paying for multiple people. I haven't come across one that has been remotely worth it.

      Review from Aaron L.

      Elite '11

      73friends

      81reviews

      Aaron L.

      New York, NY

      1/17/2011

      After living in Manhattan for a solid 7 years, I've come to realize that I'm still not a New Yorker. I ponder, from time to time, how long it will be until I graduate: how long will it be until my genetic make-up is that of a true New Yorker; how long until my birth certificate fades to nothing and reappears (in a moment of Back To The Future gusto) as one of a born New Yorker.

      The answer to the riddle, I surmised, must be in the American Museum of Natural History. If there is anywhere in New York that truly chronicles the beginnings and fate of humanity, its origins, its culture, and its soul... it would have to be the American Museum.

      The museum is itself akin to the classic contradiction of intellectuals: the science and research are sound but the presentation is lacking. While we often make this statement about the average Milhouse Nerdlinger, it is sad to have to make this statement about a gigantic museum. After all, this is a museum that houses such wonders as the world's largest meteor, the breathtaking Star of India, and the (near-) complete skeletons of Velociraptors, Tyrannosaurs, and Apatosaurus.

      In layman's terms: you'll get lost, and you just might be exhausted by the time you find what you were looking for.

      The American Museum of Natural History is a fantastic museum, make no mistake. Five floors and a wingspan of 6 New York blocks, it's filled to the brim with amazing artifacts. If you can't find something you aren't awe-inspired by, you're not human. My personal favorite was a small display of ancient European artwork: a few trinkets, a couple drawings, nothing more... except that they were crafted over 30,000 years ago. Is it not inspiring that human artwork is so innate that it predates civilization?

      I still don't know why I'm not a New Yorker, but I no longer care. I'm a human being, and this human being's anthropological heritage has been mapped out from dawn to dusk under the roof of the American Museum of Natural History. I truly wish that they had more signs and indications of where the exhibits are, and I wish they didn't deliberately confuse you about what is an exhibit and what is a store.

      That said: for visitors or residents, you should come here once a year.

      Review from Lili K.

      Lili K.

      Manhattan, NY

      1/20/2011

      WOW this place is huge!  I'm one of those museum buffs who tear through museums as fast as I can...mostly because I get bored so easily.  Yet this place is so huge, it took me 2 hours to run on through it.  

      There are a TON of exhibits, and my favorites include the dinosaurs (who can't gape in awe at the famous bones?) and beautiful landscapes with various animals in 'em (ie stuffed animals).  Yes, the animal exhibits look fake and unrealistic, but we all know it is anyways.  The backdrop, props, and scenery are quite captivating...if only I could place myself inside to pretend I joined these fierce/innocent/gorgeous animals.

      There's a little learning center for kids to play and learn hands-on style.  You can play a memory game of shells & rocks, climb into a mini tent, and stare at huge bugs that took a year to create (talk about dedication).  

      There's rooms where rats are hanging from the walls (about 15 different kinds), elephants are parading around, jewelry/stones are showing off their bling, and as always, a ripoff cafe.  There are "special" exhibits that are closed off unless you pay, such as the "brain" room and a whole room filled with sealife (and the huge whale at the center of it all).  But 'eh, I preferred paying my five cents for the suggested donation for what I got.

      A magical museum...indeed.

      Oh, and definitely stop by the little stations where you can make and send a video to yourself!  We took 3-one going up in space, another on a hot air balloon overlooking a Lion King stampede, and another in the deep blue sea.  Sweeet!

      Review from john j.

      john j.

      Norwalk, CT

      1/16/2011

      A 3 as an average of all exhibits; some are better, some worse.  

      The good: The Rose Center is spectacularly gorgeous, well-laid out, and fascinating. The Journey to the Stars Planetarium show is like actual space travel. Buying tickets at a  kiosk is a blessing because you can skip lines. We had no wait for a kiosk while everyone else was in a 15 min line.

      The Bad: For an old museum, some of the exhibits are stuck in old  modes of curatorial display. I get it, dioramas are historic, blah blah blah; but they are sorta boring. Just dead things, posed. In these areas there aren't even many signs to read. Newer exhibits like the Biodiversity Hall are better while integrating some of the older specimens. Some other regions are just poorly laid out, like the gem and mineral area. Lots of steps, levels, small walkways, dim lighting; basically you walk all the way to the dead end of the museum and can't actually read anything because everyone is up in your junk.

      The crowds: As evidenced in the newest building, the Rose Center, the problem stems from old buildings and their restrictions. The Rose Center has excellent flow, no bunching of crowds, while everywhere else you find yourself bumping into people. I don't blame the Museum for being popular, but some exhibit arrangements exacerbate the problem. Dead ends mean people are backtracking, specimen cases in the middle of the floor mean pileups. Other museums have a specific flow that fix this problem, like a one-way street. (German History Museum in Berlin, Holocaust Memorial Museum in DC).

      So overall I would give a 3. I will never forget the planetarium show, whilst I try to clear my mind of all the dead things and sweaty bodies.

      Being a native New Yorker, I was ashamed of myself for never visiting the American Museum of Natural History. I came here on MLK day since I had the day off from work. The lines for admission were, as expected, very, very long. We decided to avoid this by using the kiosk to print tickets. We chose to see the Imax film as well, Sea Rex. It was an interesting movie about the Triassic, Jurassic, and Cretaceous periods, and well worth the cost, especially for a science-inept like me. The exhibitions were aplenty. I liked seeing all the stuffed animals special to certain regions... a taxidermist's dream. The cultural exhibitions were captivating as well. I loved the Asian, Middle-Eastern, and Native-American displays especially. Within these exhibits, there were photographs and timelines of real families, a time capsule and true relic of early immigrants. I think the highlight was the top floor where all the dinosaur bones reside. It got a little crowded in terms of concentrating and really appreciating the million year old fossils, especially with tourists snapping pictures and shoulder-rubbing. Nevertheless, it was still an experience. I'd probably return on a quieter day and eat elsewhere as the food courts are expensive for menial cafeteria food.
