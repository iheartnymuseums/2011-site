      Review from Michelle L.

       

      456friends

      673reviews

      Michelle L.

      New York, NY

      11/12/2010 2 photos

      The Sony Wonder Technology Lab is free. As they used to say in
      Brooklyn  back in the day if it's free it's for me! It is good for tourists and New Yorkers alike.
      The least crowded times are, surprisingly, weekday afternoons and weekends.
      It recently went through a renovation and it  is even better now.
      In the new Sony Wonder Technology Lab  children can program robots,
      practice being in front of or behind the camera, create 3-D and motion
      capture animations, and perform open heart surgery using haptic
      technology.
      They also host family workshops, tech for tots and sci-tech workshops
      where kids can learn things like how to wire LEDs to create art and
      how to take electronics apart. All programs are free!
      Down a flight of stairs, the new play area features game stations
      where kids can try the latest in PlayStation programs, make a movie,
      play with music, and more.
      When you log out, you'll receive a certificate print-out, including a
      copy of your face print and list of what you've played/accomplished.
      I think what sets Sony Wonder apart from other children's "museums" is the hands on exhibits are subjects that they are actually interested in rather than something that adults think they should learn which is what most other children's museums have. And don't forget it's free!

      Definitely recommended for children though.

      Review from Dan B.

      Elite '11

      New York, NY

      5/25/2010

      I stumbled upon the Sony Wonder Lab in Midtown accidentally.  I was very pleased to learn that it is actually a FREE exhibit and all you need to do is put your name down for an appointment and come back at your appointed time.  Once you go up there, you're immersed in a technology-filled world with interactive exhibits and information.  My favorite part of the entire experience was near the beginning of the exhibit where you can read and learn about the entire timeline of communications from morse codes to telephonic communications to radio to cellular communications.  You learn who was responsible for each innovation in technology/communication, when it happened, and how it happened.  That was my favorite part because it was actually very interesting.

      Other than that, they have interactive exhibits like programming your own robot or making your own news report.  I found most of the interactive exhibits to be tourist-oriented which is code for mindless entertainment.  The tourists were the only bad part about the experience.  They were so brainless that they ran passed the useful and educational information and rushed straight to the animation exhibits and basically all the exhibits targeted towards morons.  Other than that, I was thankful to the Sony Wonder Lab for giving us that opportunity to enjoy these exhibits for free.  They also treated us very professionally and warmly.  You would think since it's free that they would rush you through like Gestapo police, but they were actually extremely professional and welcoming.  

      At the end of your visit, they give you a personalized photo page that shows everything you did on your visit.  Good experience.  Good way to kill a couple of hours.

      New York, NY

      6/26/2009

      I recently visited the Sony Wonder Technology Lab as part of a group to see a free screening of "Seven Pounds" on their nifty HD theater.  The theater itself is super comfortable, and they were giving out free posters for the movie at the end.  The staff was very professional and we were seated promptly and courteously.

      The lab itself is full of fun, but coming from a tech background, I think I had a different appreciation for it than most of the people I was with.  The wall that fascinated me the most was the one showing all the old technology such as old video game systems (Pong!  Atari!) and old style cameras.  They even had a display with different storage media including a ginormous floppy disk from the days of old.  This stuff is totally my cup of tea.  As much as I love current technology, I have a deep appreciation for where that technology came from.

      The high tech fun looked really neat.  Kids were having a ball doing their "fake" newscast and in another section you could create a CGI representation of yourself and have it simulate your own movements.  This seemed to get the most attention.  I was drawn to the little robots which were light sensitive and had your image on them when you swiped your badge.  My friends and I also pieced together a fake "movie trailer" which got the biggest laugh out of all of us.

      I do agree that kids will get more out of this than adults, but if you know how to let your inner child come out, this will be a fun (and FREE) time for you and like-minded friends.
