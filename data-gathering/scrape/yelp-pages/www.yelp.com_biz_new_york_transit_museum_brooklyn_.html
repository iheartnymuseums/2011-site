      Christine M.

      Queens, NY

      1/16/2011

      This is my favorite museum, I LOVE IT! I didn't expect much from this museum but after I came out of the museum, I was extremely impressed! The museum is actually underground and to enter the museum, you need go down a flight of stairs of what used to be an actual subway station. This Museum houses turnstiles, miniature bridge models, the driver's section of buses, TRAIN MODELS and a ridiculous amount of more stuff ! =D This museum is EXTREMELY informative, you can learn just about everything about New York City trains, bridges, and buses. The first topic that you will cover in the museum is the history of the subway system. You will learn how the subway system was built by reading step-by-step instructions, the dangers of the task, major events that happened during the building process, and the life of the subway builders. Afterwards you can go and learn more about the bridges and buses in general (history, problems, etc.). There is also a section on environmental issues as a result of transportation (very informative, just like the rest of the museum) and a section about NYC transit art (it was there last time I went, it might not be there anymore). The best part? When you go down, down stairs, you will enter an old subway station platform that hasn't been used for it's intended use in decades. You will see different models of the trains from different years and you can actually go inside of the trains. The advertisements and train maps that are in each train model haven't been touched since the trains last use so its really a step back into the past. I basically gave the best part of the museum away but there's a lot more to see at this museum that I haven't mentioned. It would be best to check the Museum's website to see when they are having special events.

      The admission price to enter this Museum is only $6 and what I can tell you is that all of the information that you can obtain from this Museum is worth a lot more than the ticket price. I find this museum to be very fascinating and this is DEFINITELY a place to visit no matter if you're a tourist or a New Yorker because either way you are going to use the New York City trains, bridges, buses, etc. Even though we may hate the service at times, we would all be screwed without the New York City Transit System.

      Review from Lili K.

      1/24/2011

      I love taking the subway and bus!!  Now, it can be annoying, hot, cold, there's creepers, breakdowns, etc...but for the most part, it gets you from point A to point B in a green, quick, and cheaper way than anywhere else in the states (I think).  So, why not go to a museum on NY's lovely transit system?

      It's $6, and a lot bigger than it looks (which is a good thing!).  Two floors-the bottom was an actual subway stop and has a bunch of subways-the "C" that we use already, and the old-school ones-with different colors, layouts, etc.  You can read all the ads from the past where any accessory was super cheap, and you can look into the infamous money train.  Sweeet.

      Upstairs, you can go through all the old turnstiles that they used, and swipe your card for some fun metro facts.  You can read all about the workers who risked/lost their lives building the subways, look at old bridges and signs, and try filling up gas for buses.  You can also take lovely pics of yourself driving the bus, being an attendant etc-but watch out for those kids.  A ton won't want to wait and will just jump in and play all day--now, I'm all about letting kids do what they want, but they gotta have boundaries.  There were some who were just cutting in front of other kids & their parents (and some were just ruthless).  Parents-teach your kids some boundaries, sheesh.  I made the mistake of coming here on a weekend.

      There's also a museum shop where you can buy all sorts of pricey gifts-cards, flip flops, hats, shot glasses, wallets, clutches, etc...all imprinted with the metrocard on 'em.  Now that's NY pride right there.

      This is the first public transit museum in the country and it's in Brooklyn.  

      First, the museum is inside an abandoned train station.  On the outside it looks like you are going downstairs to take the train, but nope, it will take you into a different world.  

      You will see different exhibits of how the trains were built, tools, labor, expenses, and etc.  I love the display of the different turnstiles over the years. I made an effort to go through all of them starting from the 1900s.  They also have a display of the different pay fares starting with a ticket to tokens and finally to the infamous Metrocards.  I loved how people would put washers and slugs (i.e. caps) to beat the fare.  

      The best part was downstairs with the historical trains.  It felt like riding a train from the 1950s because the model and interiors were so different.  I loved every moment of it.  I also liked hopping on board an MTA old-school bus.  

      I love how the MTA has started to incorporate more art work on their train stations with beautiful mosaics and sculptures.  

      It was a wonderful way to spend a day looking back at the history of the train system and admissions is only $6.

      Despite having the same drawbacks as many a subway station (unbearably hot, no cell service, and an apparent disapproval of pantsless frolicking), the Transit Museum rocks. If you are at all interested in the transit system and/or NYC history, it is worth the time and money ($5 for adults) it takes to visit. And if you can make it on a weekday morning, the entire area is eerily quiet.

      With all the photos and fun facts, history and hokey exhibits, my favorite part is easily the lower level, filled with previous iterations of train cars. As much as I hate contemporary commercials, I'm fascinated by advertisements from a different era; wandering between cars is like strolling through time, looking at the ads from yesteryear illuminated by bare lightbulbs. See placards pleading with you to "Buy More War Bonds!"* And proof that Heinz may actually have had 57 varieties, including their own spaghetti, and mayonnaise salad dressing, whatever the hell that is.

      On your way out, take a look at how the turnstiles have evolved over the years, and be thankful we no longer have to deal with tokens. As a nice bonus, the lovely ladies that work in the gift shop (particularly Julia) are sweethearts...perhaps the nicest and most engaged city employees I've ever encountered. They will assist you in buying all sorts of useless MTA paraphenalia, including shirts that represent whatever train you take most often.

      But seriously, you're going to wear the shirt of the train that you're on? Don't be that guy.

      Review from Jennifer N.

      Elite '11

      86friends

      489reviews

      Jennifer N.

      Washington, DC

      5/25/2010

      Let's face it- there's something magical about the NYC subway system (Jonathan Lethem's essay "Speak, Hoyt Schermerhorn" is still one of my favorites).  The Transit Museum captures that sense of magic.  

      You walk into the old subway station and through a fascinating, interactive exhibit about the construction of NYC's subway system.  And then you are face to face with  old turnstiles from various periods, and placards explaining why particular models were abandoned at particular times (also, a great collection of random slugs from other countries' currency to bottle caps!).   Ever wonder how much a subway ride was in 1945? Or what a turnstile looked like in 1961? Here's your chance to find out.

      A terrific exhibit on the magic of the Triborough Bridge was next followed by a photography exhibit from the last trip on the Myrtle Ave El.   There's even an entire back room that captures the slightly less magical NYC bus system (admittedly, the bus display didn't move me quite as much as the subway, but perhaps that's because buses are cloaked in a bit less mystery).

      But the real pay-off is on the museum's lower level where they have old subway cars that you can walk in and through (many of them also have old advertisements and old subway maps!). Also, how cute that there is a lovely fat grey cat who seems to live down there and proudly struts through the cars.

      All of this magic, all of this charm for $5.  

      My only tip - go on a weekday in the afternoon - we arrived at 1:30p and all of the school groups were leaving for the day.  We had the place to ourselves, and this allowed me to unabashedly rush through the turnstiles over and over again and to insist that my husband photograph me in the conductor's seat of a model subway.

      Review from Dan R.

      Elite '11

      152friends

      Dan R.

      Brooklyn, NY

      4/16/2010

      This place made me feel like a kid again. I'd been eager to head over to the New York Transit Museum for a while now, after having seen the advertisements for it, where else but, on the subway.

      I take the stairs underground at least twice a day, and usually on my descent, let's just say my expression is more zombie-like than that of elation. Somehow though, when going subterranean in this case happens to be the entrance to the museum, you're gonna start to get excited. And with good reason. The place itself is an actual subway station (and the cleanest you've ever seen). Tickets are bought at the station agent booth for a meager five bucks and the entire lower platform has different decades' trains on the tracks. These guys are easily the stars of the show. The doors to all the cars are open and you are free to wander in and out of bygone eras of New York City history. Make sure you check out the vintage advertisements too, chronologically consistent with each model.

      The entire museum can be explored in an hour's time, so there's no need to pick and choose one exhibit over another. See it all! The construction timeline contains some great period photography detailing the huge undertaking of building a subway system. In the same vein as the old train models, there are working turnstiles from the past for your enjoyment as well.

      The gift shop is fantastic. They have everything from toy trains to transit map cufflinks, subway line rainboots to decorative mosaic tiles from selected stations throughout the city. I wanted all of it, but due to budgetary concerns and the better judgment on my behalf not to turn my apartment into a replica of the New York underground, I fought the urge.

      The MTA gets a lot of heat, and, hey, deservedly so. But it is nice to be able to celebrate the most iconic transit system in the world. The museum is great for all ages, but it's truly best for the young ones and the young at heart.

      1) There's something to please everyone: run around like a little kid through all of the old train cars, be a nerdy history buff by reading all the informational posters, go through the hands on exhibits, bring the kids to let them run off some energy.

      2) Cheap price: $5 is not bad price. I felt it was well worth it for a non-boring, non-snobby museum experience that was very enjoyable.

      Another reason to move to Brooklyn: The Transit Museum.

      I didn't know what to expect, but it was great fun from start to finish. First of all, the fact that the museum is the old Court Street subway stop---super cool, right? You have to go downstairs to enter the museum, and there's even a second level down. The first level starts with the history of the making of the underground subway system, and from there it veers off to talk about pre-subway transport and the development of alternate mass transit forms (bridges, parkways, busses, etc).

      There's a second level (lower down) with old subway cars, which people are encouraged to walk through, to get a sense of how the cars have been modified throughout the years. I truly enjoyed the old advertisements in the cars; this is something else that's evolved (though not necessarily improved) over time.

      A lot of the exhibit speaks about the immigrant labor and how these men (of all races) worked too long and too hard for too little money. It made me appreciate the subway even more: our grandfathers and great grandfathers toiled to create this, for us. It's the Classic American Immigrant Story---sad, but inspiring.

      There's also a whole wing dedicated to Robert Moses, who was responsible for overseeing most of NYC's bridges and Parkways. He definitely deserves it.

      The best part of this museum was how hands-on it is. There were lots and lots of kids running around, and I will totally take my kids here when I meet a man to marry and have kids with. That might be a long way off, but I'm sure this museum will still be great, maybe even better.

      One more thing: This museum appeals to kids, yes, but it certainly has enough facts to satisfy adults. It is visually exciting for the youths and technically exciting for the adults (though visually exciting for adults, too).

      All in all, I really enjoyed my day here. And, since Sahadis and Damascus Bread Bakery are just around the corner I was able to eat well after my visit : )
