      8/26/2010

      This is a square look at the history of finance.  If you want Freakonomics-esque gee-whiz points, look elsewhere.  This is the fundamentals of currency, public stock, banking, and commodities.  An up to date, month-by-month history of the current recession stands out for its detail and breadth.  It's all very dry if you don't like finance, but fascinating if you do.  Money is inherently tie to numbers and meanings behind ordinary things, that can't be terribly spruced up beyond text.  Explanations could be better written, some limited instruction on the Bloomberg terminal would provide some welcome interactivity.  But it covers a great deal with above average quality, though maybe not elevating economics beyond the "dismal science."  This museum is for the enthusiasts.

      If you don't like money, you won't like this museum!

      The Museum of American Finance  will be a fascinating visit for people in the business of finance or studying finance or just interested in the "where, how and why" of the Financial world.

      The entrance and lobby is a grant spectacle of marble and granitic.
      The museum displays are interactive and informative. They cover the history of the Wall St. world from the "Curb" to the present electronic trading and the recent Wall St. meltdown; "Tracking the Credit Crisis: A Timeline."  A nice history of currency is also on display and an informative movie.  Give yourself one to two hours.

      Tip: They open late on Wednesday so I visited after work and fortunately it was free on that evening.   The only problem with going late is the lack of a live feed from the floor of the NYSE which stops after 4PM.
      Address: 48 Wall St.; Subway, J, M
      Streets are closed to cars in this area.

      Review from Eric A.

       

      17friends

      11reviews

      Eric A.

      Rego Park, NY

      3/6/2009

      Finance is one of those topics few people want to explore beyond it's utilitarian purposes. "I want to finance a car not read about finance". What people are missing out on is the fact that the history of finance is an extremely exciting topic. Including big ups and downs and lots of reversals of fortunes. Museum of American Finance captures that spirit.

      Located in the historic Bank of New York building the Museum immediately evokes that classic bank feel, before the era of ATMs and your drive through banks, when these buildings were meant to inspire a certain amount of awe. The Museum's collection is laid out on one floor and many of the exhibits offer an interactive component. Along with your traditional banking history there are sections discussing Stocks, Bond and Commodities trading. I've never seen commodities trading explained so clearly - they should do an exhibit for derivatives (no one no how to explain that). On the lower level you'll find a state of the art theater in which they'll often hold seminars and financial literacy workshops.

      Taking advantage of it's location, in the heart of the financial district, the Museum also offers a historical tour of wall street.

      All and all a great place to visit if you have an interest in the subject or are a little bit curious. You can probably enjoy the entire Museum (minus the tour) in about 2 hours.

    Specialties

    The Museum of American Finance, an affiliate of the Smithsonian Institution, is the nation's only independent public museum dedicated to celebrating the spirit of entrepreneurship and the democratic free market tradition which have made New York City the financial capital of the world.

    The Museum offers four permanent interactive exhibits: The Financial Markets, Money: A History, Banking in America and Entrepreneurs.  It also has a period room devoted Alexander Hamilton, as well as a gallery for temporary exhibits, a theater, an auditorium and a gift shop.  Current shows include "Tracking the Credit Crisis: A Timeline" and "Women of Wall Street" (through January 16, 2010).
