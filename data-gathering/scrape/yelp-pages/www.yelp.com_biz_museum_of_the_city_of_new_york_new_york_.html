      New York, NY

      2/16/2011

      The Museum of the City of New York is essentially a museum dedicated to the city and it's people. The museum's collections include paintings, drawings, prints, and photographs featuring New York City and its residents, as well as costumes, decorative objects and furniture, toys, rare books and manuscripts, marine and military collections, police and fire collections, and a theater collection documenting the golden age of Broadway theater. One of the quirky specimens in the museum is a chair belonging to Sarah Rapelje, who was reputed to be the first person born in New York!

      It's definitely worth a visit for anyone living in the city, just to help you get a better grasp of your town's history and heritage... and since it's dedicated to you, the people of New York, you might as well give it a shot!

      The museum of the City of New York is a fascinating little museum that is good for tourists to learn about the city they are visiting and also for transplants and New Yorkers to learn about the City in which they live.
      The Museum of the City of New York is a private, not-for-profit, educational agency established in 1923 to collect, preserve, and present original materials related to the history of New York City. In addition to individual contributions and gifts from foundations and corporations, the Museum receives public funds from the New York State Council on the Arts, the National Endowment for the Humanities, and the National Endowment for the Arts.
      But the jewel of this museum is the amazing Stettheimer Dollhouse: It was created in the 1920s by Carrie Stettheimer, whose artist friends reinterpreted their masterpieces in miniature to hang on the walls.
      http://www.mcny.org/mu...
      Kids Club Thirteen members receive 10% off of purchases in the Museum Shop; 2-for-1 admission on the 3th day of every month; & 10% discount on family memberships.
      Like most Museums in NYC there are programs and classes offered for children they are as follows, school programs, neighborhood explorers, Saturday academy and the Annual NYC history day.
      Like most museums in NYC they have birthday parties available for children here. They are on the pricey side but imaginative and well worth it. My favorite is the fabulous fairy tale tea party.
      My favorite part of this museum is learning  fun trivia about NYC.

      7/29/2010 1 Check-in Here

      If there are - as the clasic saying that Jay-Z recently updated goes - eight million stories in the naked city, this small museum, disappointingly, tells about three of them at a time.

      From the outside this huge place looked like it might contain many interesting exhibits (much like the Chicago History Museum back home).  Unfortunately, no (prohibition era from Hell's Kitchen) dice on the quality of the interior displays!

      Now, I might blame myself since I'm not a local.  I mean, I've been to New York more than three handfuls of times, I'm a huge history buff, and one of my grandmothers grew up in Brooklyn, but I've never *actually* lived there.  So maybe the current exhibits on former Mayor John Lindsay and the first Japanese Samurai delegation to New York in 1860 were just lost on me.  But I'd like to think that they also might be a little bit too narrowcast for anyone.  And they took up the majority of the museum at the time of my visit.  The third exhibit on the history of the automobile in New York was a bit more what I expected and way more interesting.

      Thankfully, the exhibits change out every few months but, sadly, as a tourist, I don't have the chance to explore what may take their place.

      There are a couple of interesting permanent exhibits on the second floor.  One is a, nearly museum saving, multi-media presentation called Timescape.  It traces the history of New York City through a series of maps, photographs, and paintings from pre-New Amsterdam all the way to the present.  Now, it felt a little like a propaganda piece about how New York City has lifted itself from the ashes since 1970, but I have to admit I teared up a little at the modern photos of a diverse, vibrant, five-boroughed New York City that ended it.  It made me proud to be a New Yor...

      Crap, wait, I'm not a New Yorker.  Piss bucket (which apparently is what the city smelled like under the reign of John Lindsay)!  But, seriously, it's PBS mini-series quality, only 25-minutes minutes long, and worth a viewing.

      Anyway, to be completely fair, they don't claim to be a history museum.  But I wish there was more representation of the city of New York.  I'm sure they have an impressive collection in storage but for such a big city, such a small museum just doesn't do it justice.  In my opinion it's definitely not for tourists even if it's advertised in various traveller's brochures.  The art and people across the street in Central Park will give any visitor a better representation of New York and those "exhibits" are free.

      I'm not a museum guy, but since moving to New York (and falling in love with New York), I have really wanted to check out the Museum of the City of New York.  Not being a museum guy, I wasn't aware that you can't do a museum in 45 minutes.  (They close at 5:00, and I got there around 4:15.)  I figured that the museum would have some sort of planned path that you are supposed to follow, and maybe even headphones that tell you about the exhibits.  That's the way that museums always seem to be on TV.

      Anyway, this museum wasn't like that.  It was more of a free-form, walk around and look at whatever you want type of deal.  The girl who was collecting admission suggested that I start with the Charles Addams exhibit.  So I did.  I thought that was absolutely incredible.  I never even knew that The Addams Family was created by a guy named Addams.  So I had no idea that he was a cartoonist.

      The exhibit had a ton of Addams' original drawings, and a few of his preliminary sketches.  I particularly appreciated one example in which they had both the preliminary sketch and the original drawing for the same cartoon.

      The exhibit also pointed out Addams' attention to detail as far as architecture is concerned.  The point was that the realism of the New York backdrops often made the hidden jokes in his cartoons a little more subtle.  Since the architecture of New York is one of my favorite parts of the city, I really enjoyed seeing Addams' representations of it.

      I also checked out the "Cars, Culture and the City" exhibit for a few minutes.  It didn't look as interesting as the Charles Addams exhibit, but I really only had about 10 minutes to rush through it.  So I'm guessing that I would have liked it a lot more if I had given myself a little more time.

      Overall, I loved the museum, and I definitely plan to go back there at some point when I can drag myself across Central Park a little earlier in the day.

      Review from Rita E.

      Elite '11

      60friends

      Rita E.

      Brooklyn, NY

      11/3/2009

      A hidden gem!

      Located on an overlooked stretch of Museum Mile, this stately museum is small compared to behemoths like the Met or the Guggenheim but has rich, interesting exhibits regarding New York in many aspects, both historical and contemporary. They range from photography exhibits (bombed out buildings in the South Bronx in the '80s, the current Legacy exhibit on wild landscapes in NYC -- fabulous!) to costumes (Paris/NYC in the 1920s) to environmental (Greening New York), to the history of trade in New York, etc.

      It's small enough to not be overwhelming, but large enough to have many exhibits at once.

      I come here most often for the public lectures; they have lecture series and panels based on current exhibits, some of which are free and some of which are ticketed, non-members $12 and members $6. They do charge for 'walk-ins' if you haven't reserved a ticket though, which is a shame because I don't believe they're ever packed to capacity, at least when I've been there. The crowd at the lectures skews a little older but I've seen a mix of some younger people (20s/30s) too.

      MCNY hasn't been great about advertising themselves, I didn't even know this place existed until a couple of years ago.

      Bottom line: If you're looking for an affordable alternative to the mobs a little further down 5th Avenue and want to learn some cool stuff about New York City's past and present, check out MCNY.

      Gourmet G.

      San Francisco, CA

      8/1/2005 1 photo

      It opened in relatively small quarters in Gracie Mansion in 1923, but its rapid growth and increasing popularity with the public warranted a move in 1932 to its current location on the stretch of Fifth Avenue now known as Museum Mile. Although its permanent collection of paintings, prints, maps, and photographs is impressive, the museum is best known for temporary exhibits focusing on the cultural history of the city. Past shows have focused on everything from Coney Island past and present to a tribute to the sadly defunct Horn & Hardart Automat, complete with an original wall of windowed doors behind which the various food selections were displayed. It also houses one of the most extensive collections of theatrical costumes, set pieces, posters, and decorative objects in the country. Be sure to visit the section devoted to antique toys. A small gift shop has an outstanding selection of books, artwork, and assorted NYC memorabilia.

      Review from Shar B.

      Shar B.

      5/15/2009

      This museum is terrific & fascinating.  Its smaller size & lesser-known status makes for a relaxing day, with less of a population to contend with than other larger, more popular museums (I adore The Met & MOMA but sometimes ack, all those people...) & a more "doable" (less overwhelming) collection of stuff to take in.

      The exhibits here appeal to all sorts of lovers & appreciators of New York City.  Some of the exhibits are sweeping collections, such as the current one on the "World of Henry Hudson" (saw just this past Mother's Day) - paintings, maps, documents, objects & so forth of the period, conveying immense details & garnering a NY Times review (I got a personal kick when I picked up a listening device that played some shipman's traditional song... didn't know how to stop it playing so I hung up the phone & heard the song still playing across the large exhibition room for like 15 minutes).  Other exhibits focus on smaller aspects of NYC, such as strikingly beautiful paintings of NYC stoops focusing on the details of swirling iron railings & resulting plays of sun/shadow (complete w/ the addresses of each stoop).  The multimedia 1/2 hour long "Timescapes movie" (triple screened w/ assorted photos & maps) was rich w/ cool NYC history.  In the past, I've seen the cool toy exhibit, or ones about performance, immigration, etc...

      Admission is pay what you wish but a "suggested" $10... really it's worthwhile giving at least several bucks to keep this awesome place chugging along, if you can't (or choose not to) give the full suggested price.  They do have a dealio where if you say "I'm from the neighborhood" (East Harlem) you get in free.

      The museum is also right next to Central Park's lovely Conservatory Garden (which, like the museum, is lovely without being SO big it takes up all day); visiting both in one day surely makes for one of those fabulous NYC afternoons!
