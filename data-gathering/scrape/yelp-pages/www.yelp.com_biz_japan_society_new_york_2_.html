      The trek here is a walk if you're coming from the train, or even the bus. Well for one thing, I do take Japanese classes here.. but am also a member of the society. There are interesting lectures at times, and when you're hot.. walking into the building and hearing the man made waterfall running.. it is an interesting thing to sit through.       There are lots of areas within learning about the culture of Japan.. so Japan Society provides a bit for learning parts about it.

      There are about four levels to this building. The top part is a gallery or museum area. The main level is where there the box office, or lecture/party area with the waterfall. The area lower is the auditorium, bathroom.. while the bottom level is the language center for learning Japanese.

      Everyone talks about the Japanese toilets, and yes they are Toto Washlets... what ele to talk about it? Hmm... I enjoy the automatic soap dispenser and the faucet. One thing I do wish is that they would have hand dryers.. I keep on looking at the towels being used and I used them myself.. and think hmm.. Japanese bathroom... hand dryer..

      I, too, was here for Murakami's Little Boy exhibit, and was frankly amazed that such an upscale and well-respected establishment would treat anime, toys, and sketches with such respect and pride.

      The curation and layout of the entire exhibit was enormously informative, well organized, and visually attractive.       Nina C.

      1/16/2008

      I've always known that the Japan Society was around, but I guess I never had much interest to come on in and poke around...

      Until I heard about the Making a Home exhibit.

      And even then, I was a bit hesitant to go.  What do I know about art anyways?  I look, I like, I don't, I leave.  That's pretty much the extent of my art knowledge.  You can go on and on about post-impressionist this and Dali-esque that and I'd have no idea what you were talking about...  Moving on.

      I had read about the exhibit and thought it might be interesting - especially the ON Megumi Akiyoshi room and Yoko Ono's wishing tree.  Miguel and I met up and headed to the JS together.

      I was impressed with a lot of the pieces in the exhibit.  ON Megumi Akiyoshi's room was spectacularly brilliant in it's colors.  Slightly disappointed that Yoko Ono's wishing tree was so small, but impressed with her board dedicated to mothers.  Both of Ono's pieces were interactive, meaning you could write a wish and tie it on the tree or write a message to your Mom right on the board.

      After exploring all of the JS from top to bottom, we decided to head out.  And what else could we do, but finish the night with some Japanese izakaya grub?  Num num.

      Review from Miguel d.

       

      140friends

      216reviews

      Miguel d.

      Brooklyn, NY

      12/27/2007

      I saw the Making a Home exhibit at Japan Society, with many installations focusing on usage of space, loneliness, and relevance to a new environment. It was free on Friday night, and after a fun bumpy ride on the M15, we ended up outside of the United Nations Plaza en route to the Japan Society.

      The event was well organized and very captivating. The flow of the works was well thought out and, as museums should, introduce questions and make you wonder at the scale of work that the artists do.

      Nina was there mainly for the On Megumi Akiyoshi room, which was a splendor in pink and other gorgeously bright colors; I really wanted to see Yoko Ono's two installations. Her "mother board" was a giant blank wall where patrons could write messages to their mothers. Right next to that was her wishing tree, where people from all over can make a wish by tying their wish on a branch.

      We were super excited about the prospect of going to Choux Factory immediately afterward but they had just closed. Darn you holiday hours!

      I would like to see a few more things here before writing a rave review.  I came here the other day to see a performance called Five Days in March.  I meant to come here from before, but never got around to it.

      The place is very beautiful and clean.  The shop on the second floor has some very cool stuff that you can't find in other Japanese specialty stores, including books and merchandise that are related to their past exhibits.

      The theater space was very nice and comfortable.  The seats were nice, and anybody from any position can see the stage well.  And the bathrooms at the theater level are of course equipped with those high-tech Japanese toilets.  My parents' house in Japan actually has one of those, and it flushes itself too, so I am not easily impressed.

    About This Business

    Provided by business

    Specialties

    art, business, culture, education, film, innovation, language, lectures, performing arts, policy.

    History

    Established in 1907

    Founded when few Americans knew anything about Japan, Japan Society was a pioneer of cultural exchange in the early 20th century. Today it has evolved into North America's major producer of high-quality content on Japan for an English-speaking audience. With over 100 events annually in the arts, business, education, innovation and policy, the Society is a world renowned nonprofit, nonpolitical organization that provides access to information on Japan, offers opportunities to experience Japanese culture, and fosters sustained and open dialogue on issues important to the U.S., Japan, and East Asia.

    Japan Society's landmark building by architect Junzo Yoshimura opened in 1971 as the first building by a leading Japanese architect in New York City. Located near the United Nations activities at Japan Society are set against a stunning backdrop of indoor gardens, a reflecting pool and a waterfall. Facilities include a 262-seat theater, art gallery, language center, and conference facilities.
