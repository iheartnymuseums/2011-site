      It's a walk through history!  You'll experience & appreciate everything that all immigrates had to go through when they entered the U.S. back in our past history.  Everything that you ever read in your U.S. history books and all the archival footage that you've seen in class or on documentaries are located in this building.

      It's pretty cool to see and be in the actual room where immigrants had to enter & register.  It's looks the same in the black & white photos in our history books.  The thing that I really appreciated about this place is that it talks about more than just Ellis Island, but all over the U.S. and their immigration history; including the Bay Area where I grew up.  My parents were born in China, so they had their own unique experience immigrating to the West Coast.

      As for the immigration history that they display, they show the beauty of inviting all religion & cultures, including the ugliness and racism in our immigration's history.  They had a huge section where they honor all the different cultures that had made up the U.S. as it is today; including a tree that represents all these American words that originated from their native country.  They also have a pretty cool computer where they show exactly how many specific ethnicity are located in the entire U.S. and their specific states.  You'll find out exactly who are the minorities around our country.

      If you are in the mood to experience and appreciate our history, this is the place to check out when you're in New York.  This is history that comes to life!

      Humbling and impressive all at once. As you approach the island, you get some sense of what it might have been like for someone coming to America. You sail past the Statue of Liberty, you can see the Manhattan skyline in the distance, then you're greeted by several large buildings as pull into the slip.

      Once off the boat, you enter the main building by going up a walkway and steps. The size of the entry room is overwhelming - a high ceiling and lots of space. It was likely very crowded back when the building was operating.

      They have lots of displays, lots of pictures, audio accounts from immigrants who entered through the island. You'll spend hours here so don't think you'll just check this off your list as a last minute thing to do.

      I wish more of the buildings were open so you could get a sense of the entire experience. The island seems large and across from the main building are a series of buildings that were used as hospitals and more.

      There's an area on the main floor where you can go and look up passenger lists from boats that entered Ellis Island. You can look up your family name. It probably works better if you go in with a list of relative's names since you only get about 20 minutes to do the research. When you're done they give you a code so you can do more from your home computer.

      Ed U.

      San Francisco, CA

      1/11/2009 11 photos

      Gee, if you think the line at Grimaldi's Pizza can get bad, imagine how it must have felt to be a new arrival from the Old World at the turn of the last century. You with your six kids, your hump-backed wife (the poor thing), and one moth-eaten bag since you were allowed only one piece of luggage to enter the country. Sunrise ...sunset...swiftly flow the days. Oh wait, that was "Fiddler on the Roof". But with this "You Are There"-type of museum, you do get a strong sense of what the immigrant experience was like before people went by smug customs inspectors' furrowed brows at an airport.

      For most tourists like myself, this is Part Two of the Statue of Liberty-Ellis Island boat cruise where for a worthwhile twenty bucks, you can walk around Lady Liberty and then take the boat again to Ellis Island where you see the grandiose Main Building where most of the U.S. citizenry, other than Native Americans and native Americans, entered these shores officially. My ancestors came from the other direction, but to the curators' credit, they have made a point of being thorough in their presentation as there is a lot of information about Angel Island in San Francisco Bay as well as other points of entry.

      Do yourself a favor and take the museum's self-guided audio tour which is on the comprehensive side but does allow you to stop at all the exhibits chronicling the full immigrant experience at Ellis Island and includes artifacts, photographs, prints, videos, interactive displays, oral histories, and temporary exhibits. But for goodness sake, make sure to pre-pay at Castle Clinton in Battery Park where you get the package deal for both Liberty and Ellis Islands. I don't think it's all that clear from the outset, so don't tell me I didn't forewarn you.

      Re-opened in 1990, the 40,000-square-foot Main Building is the big attraction as it should be after a meticulous restoration done in the 1980's. The main hall on the second floor is particularly impressive in its scope. On the audio tour, you can hear actors giving voice to the immigrants themselves and their stories of hope. Even though the fill Liberty-Ellis Island excursion takes a good part of a day, this is definitely worth a trip if you've never been. I uploaded several photos in the gallery to whet your appetite. Let's play Neil Sedaka's "The Immigrant", shall we? ...http://www.youtube.com...
