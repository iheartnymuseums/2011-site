      At the risk of facing major hate from folks, I both pity and am amazed at the Brooklyn Museum. On the one hand, it is the Metropolitan-lite, serving as an encyclopedic museum. On the other hand, it is also trying very hard to be hip and edgy with their newer, temporary exhibits.

      The result? I LIKE the museum, and their respective exhibits are great, but something feels a bit off, especially after their renovation. I feel guilty saying this, because aesthetically, the redesigns are great and to my taste (read: crazy colors!), but I feel like they could try to unite their two disparate "themes" more coherently, whether through architecture or spatial layout.

It's not a place I would go out of my way for, given my location in Queens, but it's definitely a magnum opus for Brooklyn and would LOVE to have if there was a version in Queens. Oy.

      Review from Kiki M.

      Kiki M.

      Seattle, WA

      9/26/2010

      I had the desire to visit MoMA or the Guggenheim but my jam packed schedule wouldn't quite allow it plus I was hesitant to pay a $20 admission fee. What can I say? I'm cheap! Now the Brooklyn Museum was a place I could make time for because I was in Brooklyn, it takes a reasonable $10 to get in, and your museum ticket can get you into the Brooklyn Botanic Garden for free if used the same day.

      The Brooklyn Museum is in a striking building with a staircase and water works to gawk at. It was a sunny day when I went and many took advantage reading or eating lunch on the steps and children took pleasure in guessing where the water would sprout from.

      There are five floors and I, unfortunately, was unable to visit them all.  The lobby is a typical museum lobby, plain with an admissions desk. I purchase my ticket and wander to the first exhibit which was art from a variety of Asian, African, and Middle Eastern countries. Up the slow moving elevator and there are recreated rooms from the Civil War area. Kiki Smith added her own touch with bigger than life paper mache female figures in some rooms. As I continued to another floor there is an archive room filled with objects that ranged from firearms to armchairs to dishes to statues. There was an exhibit with jewelry created by the metal worker Art Smith and a feminist wing.

      I made it through the museum at almost light speed and that hindered me greatly from taking time to really absorb all the art but it was varied and each floor kept me wanting to see more.

      There is a gift shop and a cafe that had a similar feel to the IKEA cafeteria. I didn't eat there but as I browsed the menu you have the option of cold or hot entrees. The gift shop wasn't any different that your usual museum gift shop: books, cards, jewelry, items pertaining to certain exhibits.

      My only qualm about the museum was there were a few sections that were closed off due to either construction or a new art was being readied to display. I couldn't find stairs to get to the other floors as a result. The elevator took some time to get to whatever floor I was on and instead of taking me directly to the floor I requested it stopped and opened at each floor until I reached my desired destination. But it was only a minor annoyance.

      The Brooklyn Museum is well worth the visit and it is a great price!

      Review from John W.

      Elite '11

      82friends

      132reviews

      John W.

      Queens, NY

      8/10/2010 11 photos 2 Check-ins Here

      Here's your basic litmus test for an art museum.  If you're there for about 3 hours, and start to get a slight headache from sensory overload, it's probably a good museum.  That, or you're in a place that doubles as a laser tag arena.

      Fortunately, there's no sign of laser tag, and the Brooklyn Museum certainly delivers that blissful feeling of artistic overwhelm.  You could potentially explore all areas of the museum in a single (albeit, long) visit, which is impossible for a much larger place like the Met or even MoMA, but I feel it's always best to just let things sink in, and save the rest for next time.

      Next time is a really easy proposition, as they have FREE evenings every first Saturday of the month (starting at 5pm).  The good folks at Target are very generous sponsors, and the museum generally schedules a whole barrage of special events on each first Saturday--discussions, music, dancing, films, and so on.  And even if you don't go on a first Saturday, if you want to avoid the crowd, the regular admission is very reasonable.  You always get your money's-worth at the Met because it's so huge, but with other places like the Guggenheim and the Whitney, you can feel a little gypped if you paid $20 and were moved by NOTHING (this does happen on occasion).  

      Another nice plus of the Brooklyn Museum--they're one of the few museums I've visited that allows personal photography in just about every single area.  I'm sure there are special exhibits from time to time where this is not allowed, but for the most part it is, and that makes for such a rich experience.  I love to share my experiences (that's why I Yelp, after all...), and this makes it so easy.

      Hmmmmmm, let's see, what else....

      FREE WIFI on the plaza, that actually works, WELL.

      Great gift shop, with all kinds of cool merchandise, some of which you wouldn't see in a more "traditional" art museum gift shop.

      A really nice building.  There's some debate among BM purists about the glass exterior that was added several years back, but I for one LOVE the mix of old world/new world you feel when you walk in the front door.

      Staff EVERYWHERE.  And not that snooty, stereotypical art museum security, that looks at you funny if you get within 10 feet of the Warhol mural on the wall.  Somehow, these people seem more real, more down to earth.

      God, there must be so much else that I'm just forgetting right now.  Bottom line is, this is one of the best, most complete museums I've been to.  It doesn't get mobbed with tourists and people who don't look like they know what they're looking at (even when the free Saturdays draw large crowds).  And it's really easy to get to--if you're in midtown Manhattan, you can be here in 20 minutes, easily.  Plus, this is one of my favorite neighborhoods in the world, so making a day of your visit is 100% justified.

      Review from Michael G.

      Elite '11

      302friends

      397reviews

      Michael G.

      Chicago, IL

      7/28/2010 6 photos 1 Check-in Here

      While I know this isn't something to necessarily be proud of, I live my life connected to the internet. If you ever see me in person, even if I'm being totally social, my smart phone is glued to my hand to keep me connected to the outside world. I guess I'm sort of a prototype for a cyborg. I have to be connected to the internet at all times in order to download the constant stream of information I might miss, and in turn end up malfunctioning, otherwise.

      And, let me tell you, this prototype is one happy camper (but wait, oh my goodness, that won't happen, what if there's no 3G available?) at the Brooklyn Museum. It's a place that read my (cyber)mind.

      I don't know if any of my New York City peeps have noticed this, but internet doesn't work in most of your museums. It's like you're supposed to be disconnected from the world while appreciating art (I'm looking at you Guggenheim - jerks!). Not at this one. Not only does the Brooklyn have free wi-fi all the way out into the park out front and to the scale model of the Statue of Liberty in the parking lot, inside is wired as one giant hotspot.

      But this isn't just so people can surf while their friends or significant other look at pieces they don't care about (I mean, what dude actually likes all art?). The Brooklyn Museum has cataloged many of their pieces into a giant online database (http://www.brooklynmus...) and while visitors stroll through the museum, they can grab the catalog number off the description cards and "like" or even leave comments on a piece. Like enough of them and the museum makes recommendations of what piece to see next!

      And while my phone's crappy battery life didn't allow me to tag enough to take advantage of this, I could see why this would be necessary. The Brooklyn Museum is well worth leaving the tourist comfort zone of midtown Manhattan for with its gigantic collection which ranges from Egyptian mummies (watch what time you go or it will be overrun by childlings) to post-modern feminist art (how many ways can a self-portrait of the lady parts be painted). And almost none of it is stuffy (there is one classical European art room that I found yawny) in the multiple hours I spent there taking in only a small portion.

      It's not a stuffy museum spatially either. The high vaulted ceilings and open spaces make this a very inviting and welcoming space. Unless you're easily offended.

      I had almost forgotten before a couple of friends reminded me that the Brooklyn Museum is never afraid to invite controversy by what its pieces portray. In that way, the Brooklyn is also a museum after my own mind. Though that's, maybe, also a personality trait I shouldn't be proud of. But that thought's a whole other review.

      Review from Tom K.

      Tom K.

      Forest Hills, NY

      7/20/2010

      Even before entering the actual museum, I was taken aback by the building itself. Names of scientists and religious figures adorned the exterior, along with several statues. This Brooklyn Beaux-Arts beauty of a building dates back to 1897.

      The recommended donation is $10 for non-students, non-seniors and non-children. I fall into that category and considering that the museum captures a microcosm of the world, an admission of $10 was reasonable. Hey, I was also receiving air-conditioning which was much needed on this rather hot Saturday afternoon.

      But I do not exaggerate when claiming that the world of art is at your fingertips here. I was especially pleased by the first floor African art section that featured genuine Dogon masks of their creator gods along with a video of their funeral rites. You can move from African art to Andy Warhol all on the same floor-- talk about context shifts!

      Fans of preserved house interiors will not be disappointed by the Brooklyn Museum's Schenck houses that capture what life was like for an early Dutch settler. The house's bright red exterior was surprising as I usually envision that era as dour and earth toned.

      Finally, no mention of the museum is complete without a shout out to the Egyptian collection. If you like MILFS..(Mummies I'd like to fotograph-- get your mind out of the pyramid gutter, please.) then this area is for you. Passages from the Egyptian Book of the Dead are on display for the curious.

      I also have to mention the impressive collection of American art, from colonial to modern.

      In many ways , the Brooklyn Museum is a mini-Met and I mean that as the highest of compliments.

      And the Italian sausage sandwich from the outdoor halal truck was very tasty-- on a bun or pita, for those who work up an appetite for food after sampling the cultural delights.

    History

    Established in 1897

    The Brooklyn Institute of Arts and Sciences, the forebear of the Brooklyn Museum, was conceived in the nineteenth century as the focal point of a planned cultural, recreational, and educational district for the burgeoning city of Brooklyn. Although the scope of that envisioned complex of parks, gardens, and buildings changed after the once-independent Brooklyn was absorbed into New York City in 1898, many features of the plan were eventually realized and are reflected in what can be seen today. In the area of land once designated as the Brooklyn Institute Triangle can be found not only the Brooklyn Museum but also such other institutions and facilities as the Brooklyn Botanic Garden, the Prospect Park Zoo, Mount Prospect Park, and the Central Library of the Brooklyn Public Library system. Just beyond the western edge of the Institute Triangle complex stands the monumental entrance to Prospect Park, marked by the Soldiers' and Sailors' Memorial Arch in the center of Grand Army Plaza.
