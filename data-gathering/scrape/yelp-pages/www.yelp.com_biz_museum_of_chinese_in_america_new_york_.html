      Review from Warren L.

       

      1friend

      39reviews

      Warren L.

      Queens, NY

      8/7/2010

      New York has an embarrassment of riches when it comes to museums and MOCA is one that packs the most into a relatively small space.

      Other reviewers note that it isn't very large, and that's true, but for me it only means I wish it were larger and telling more stories.  There are three general functional parts of the museum: a large and lengthy display on the history of the Chinese in the United States: immigrants, objects of racism and very overt legal discrimination, and success both with and without assimilation.  The second is a collection of short bios of famous and obscure Chinese who made significant contributions to American culture and society.  The third is a display of a Chinese general goods store from another age.

      MOCA holds many public programs and it's worth becoming a member both to support their work and to keep in touch with the programs.

      Every New Yorker should visit, along with the tourists.  And when reading through the sections about laws requiring Chinese residents to carry identification papers to prove their legal status ask yourself "So, we've been here and done that before?"

      Review from Matteo R.

      Elite '11

      283friends

      368reviews

      Matteo R.

      Brooklyn, NY

      2/14/2010 7 photos

      This is a wonderful exhibition space dedicated to celebrating and honoring the contributions and struggles of Chinese Americans[1].

      The space itself, truly beautiful in different shades of wood and exposed bricks, was designed by Maya Lin[2]; the façade of wood, concrete and bronze is both elegant and beautiful. The museum, which actually has a second entrance on Lafayette St., extends on two floors. The first floor is dedicated to the history and struggles of Chinese Americans, and on the second floor downstairs there is a computer room and the restrooms.

      At ground level you can find interactive touch screen TVs, plus a lot of notes and memorabilia, including historical film footage. I particularly enjoyed the ones about actress Anna May Wong (a personal favorite) and entrepreneur Charlie Toy, with a voiceover reading excerpts from their own writings. The part describing racial discrimination in Hollywood and in the media in the first half of the past century is powerful and chilly (and still very actual). I also learned that it was thanks to Wong Kim Ark in 1898 that the 14th amendment now extends the right of citizenship to all people born in the United States, independently of their race, color,  religion, or belief.

      On the first floor there is also the conference room, and a large room dedicated to contemporary artists. I particularly liked a piece by  Hung-Chih Peng[3]: a film shows a wall with excerpts of the Bible painted on it in Arabic translations; a dog licks off the writings, but the film is shown in reverse, so that it appears that the dog is actually writing the excerpts.

      I recently came here with my gf on Valentine's Day/Chinese New Year's Day for a dumpling making workshop. The workshop, which took place in the floor below street level, was a lot of fun: with the help of chef Wai Hon Chu[4], we got to make (and eat!) sticky rice dumplings stuffed with sesame paste. I also learned how the hell the soup is put inside xiao long bao[5].

      Check out the gift shop: together with fun stuff like cards, paper lamps, hoodies, and tee-shirts, they also have quite a lot of books, including children books and cooking books.

      Free admission on Thursday.

      Review from Jennifer N.

      Jennifer N.

      Washington, DC

      11/2/2009

      We went because we love Maya Lin, and we couldn't miss an opportunity to see her work.  The space is beautiful in its quiet elegance, and the curators have worked to pack a lot of historical information into a relatively small space.

      A few notes:
      1) It's not a museum- it's more of an exhibit space (which is to say it's not like Asia Society, or even the Tenement Museum).  MOCA doesn't really have any artifacts they are displaying (with a few exceptions).  More than anything, the exhibit gives visitors a broad overview of the history of Chinese America.
      2) It's REALLY dark in there, and it's often hard to read the placards.
      3) There's a lot packed into a very small space- the entire museum probably consists of five or six galleries, and it feels like every inch of wall space is taken up with information.  It's all done so beautifully, but the truth is it can almost feel overwhelming to be surrounded by so much.

      Even with all these caveats, it's still a beautiful space and worth a visit.

      Review from Cheryl C.

      Cheryl C.

      Chicago, IL

      6/18/2007

      If you're not looking for this cute little gem, you'll miss it for sure. It's located in a community center, so you'll have to go up an ancient staircase and down a disorderly hallway full of children in martial arts uniforms. Go through the marked door and tell them you're there for the museum. They'll be happy to see you. Suggested admission, $2 for adults, $1 for students and seniors, free on Fridays.

      The "museum" is a single, initially unimpressive room, but take a closer look and you can see all the love and care that's gone into presenting these artifacts. There are tiny shoes, traditional dress and lion masks on display, but the real draw is the evidence of what life was like for early Chinese immigrants. There are pieces of the printing presses from Chinese newspapers; irons and signs from Chinese laundries; letters home to China in both English and Chinese. Also, there's an ongoing exhibit devoted to the experience of Chinese Americans surrounding 9/11. Docents are very helpful and engaging.

      I recommend this museum to anyone looking for something a little out of the ordinary to do ... or just a way to sneak in a little civic virtue between your dumplings and your bubble tea.

      Has been a couple of years since I visited MoCA last, but they make great use of the small space that they have (stay tuned as they're on the quest to open in a larger, handicapped-accessible location!) With exhibits like, "Have You Eaten Yet?" (an exhibit on the Chinese restaurant which is now touring nationally!) MoCA professionally and creatively explores different facets of Chinese American history.

      The museum is laid out into two galleries, with a small wall for an additional 2-D installation by the front entrance. The first gallery is their permanent exhibition, "Where Is Home?" that explores concepts of home for Chinese immigrants and American born Chinese. With objects from the everyday lives of Chinese folks - including many items that were salvaged from the streets of NY Chinatown - you begin to build a concept of where, or what, home is to you.

      Good sense of local history, too -- MoCA offers walking tours and staff can make recommendations as to other things to see in NY Chinatown. Ask about Chinese Cuban restaurants!

      Great museum.  I thought the new location's design by Maya Lin was incredible - she really outdid herself.  The recreation of the Chinatown general store is awesome.  And I loved the profiles of prominent Chinese Americans that were displayed on the walls.  What I especially loved was that they paid attention to the political history of Chinese Americans.  For those who do not know much about Chinese American history, this is a great tour.  For those who do know a bit of Chinese American history, the museum is a great visual display and exhibit of that history!
